## 1. The question this review is answering

Ride-hailing platforms make three operational decisions on a spatiotemporal graph of a city: **whom to assign to whom (matching)**, **what to charge (pricing)**, and **where idle drivers should go (rebalancing)**. These decisions are coupled — pricing changes demand, demand shapes matching feasibility, and matching outcomes determine the post-trip idle distribution that rebalancing then has to correct. The question this review is answering is: *what does the 2018–2026 ML and OR literature on these three decisions look like, separately and jointly, and where are the bridges and gaps between them?*

The five threads from `ride-share-deep-research.md` are kept as a coverage device but are not used as the organizing principle of the prose, which is theme-first by design.

### 1.1 Search strategy, inclusion criteria, and bounded-out adjacent literatures

**Databases and time window.** The corpus was assembled by (a) seeding from `ride-share-deep-research.md`, an in-house deep-research compilation; (b) cross-checking against arXiv (2018–April 2026), ACM Digital Library (KDD, CIKM, WWW, AAAI), PMLR (ICML, L4DC), IEEE Xplore (CDC, ECC, T-ITS, TCNS), INFORMS journals (Management Science, MSOM, Journal on Applied Analytics), and **Econometrica (Wiley)** [v3 phrasing fix]. The default time window is 2018–2026, with explicit carve-outs for landmark older works that seed reviews cite (e.g., Lin 2018, Xu 2018).

**Keyword set.** "Ride-hailing" OR "ride-sourcing" OR "AMoD" OR "autonomous mobility on demand" OR "robo-taxi" — combined with — "deep reinforcement learning" OR "MARL" OR "multi-agent" OR "graph neural network" OR "GNN" OR "foundation model" OR "LLM" OR "large language model" — combined with — "matching" OR "dispatching" OR "rebalancing" OR "repositioning" OR "fleet management" OR "pricing" OR "surge".

**Inclusion criteria.** Peer-reviewed papers in the venues above plus arXiv preprints with claim-level evidence (a clearly stated method, a defined dataset, and a numeric result). Position papers and tutorials were excluded. Where multiple versions of a paper exist (preprint + journal), the most recent peer-reviewed version is cited.

**Corpus size.** 27 papers — **below the guide's own 30–50 target.** The shortfall is acknowledged and is the single largest unresolved scope critique of v3. Section 11 names 6–10 specific seed citations whose addition would bring the corpus to 33–37 and would either harden or contest Themes 4 and 6 (whose current evidence base is too thin to support field-level claims). Until v4 expansion lands, theme-level claims about "the field" are scoped to the corpus reviewed, and three theme paragraphs (Themes 4, 6, and the controversy/gap framings of each) are downgraded to corpus-coverage observations.

**Bounded-out adjacent literatures (and why).** A senior reviewer in this area would expect the following five literatures to appear; each is bounded out of v3 with a defense, and seed citations are listed in Section 11.

1. **Mechanism-design and incentive-compatible spatial pricing** (Ma, Fang, Parkes and follow-ons). Bounded out because the review's pricing thread is anchored on the welfare/equilibrium question (does spatial pricing improve total surplus and how is it distributed?), and IC mechanism design answers a different question (does the pricing rule incentivize truthful reporting?). The two are complements rather than substitutes; v4 should add an IC-pricing sub-thread to Theme 4.
2. **Mean-field-game-aware reinforcement learning** (Guo et al.; Saldi; Yang Yaodong's MFG-RL line). Bounded out **partly in error** in v1 — this is the literature that bridges OR equilibrium pricing and DRL control, and Theme 4's controversy claim ("DRL pricing is not in dialogue with OR/economics") is therefore overstated. v3 explicitly downgrades Theme 4 from controversy to coverage gap pending v4 expansion. v4 should treat MFG-RL as a sixth thread.
3. **Ride-pooling / shared rides beyond BMG-Q** (Alonso-Mora et al. 2017 PNAS framework and descendants). Bounded out because pooling adds a capacity-constraint dimension orthogonal to the matching/pricing/rebalancing axes that organize this review. Hu2025 BMG-Q is included because it is RL-side and graph-attention; the optimization-side pooling literature is not, which leaves the BMG-Q comparison "unmoored" (the senior reviewer's term) until the OR-side pooling literature is added.
4. **Driver labor economics** (Hall, Horton, Knoepfle on Uber labor supply; Cook et al. on the Uber gender earnings gap). Bounded out because the corpus is focused on platform-side control, not driver-side empirical labor questions. The strategic-driver assumption that Theme 4 wants DRL to adopt is empirically grounded in this literature, however, and v4 should cite at least Hall–Horton–Knoepfle as motivation, otherwise §8's "adopt strategic agents" recommendation is methodologically circular (only OR papers are cited as evidence for the OR assumption).
5. **ST-GNN demand-forecasting backbone** (DCRNN, Graph WaveNet, STGCN). Bounded out because the review's GNN-encoder discussion (Theme 2) is about *control* representations, not forecasting. The forecasting literature feeds in via UniST/UrbanGPT but is not separately threaded.

**Geographic and platform scope.** The empirical portion of the corpus is heavily concentrated on Didi (Xu 2018, Eshkevari 2022, Yang 2024, Yue 2024, Zhang 2024 NondBREM — five of six deployment/empirical papers), Lyft (Azagirre 2024 — one paper), and NYC-taxi-derived simulators (Lin 2018, Hu 2025, Tresca 2026, Han 2025, Lyu 2025, Yuan 2024, Li 2024). Bolt / FreeNow (EU), Ola (India), Grab / Gojek (Southeast Asia), and Didi-Brazil deployments are not represented. The Castillo 2019/2025 welfare result is from a single market (Houston). Where empirical claims are presented in Sections 4–8, this geographic concentration is flagged and claims are scoped accordingly.

## 4. Field overview (background paragraph)

The 2018–2026 ride-hailing ML literature in this corpus has converged on **deep RL with GNN encoders and bipartite-matching combinatorial solvers** as the dominant matching/rebalancing stack, with a fast-growing **LLM/foundation-model** layer on top and a parallel **OR/economics pricing equilibrium** track that has only partially intersected with the ML side within this corpus. The empirical center of gravity in dispatching shifted from single-agent value learning (Xu2018) to multi-agent RL with mean-field approximations (Li2019, Hao2023) and then to graph-attention MARL with grid-as-agent or vehicle-as-agent variants (Wang2025, Hu2025). In autonomous mobility-on-demand the bi-level GNN-RL + linear-program template (Gammelli2021, Gammelli2023, Singhal2024, Tresca2026) is dominant; in deployed dispatch, the production lineage at Didi (Xu2018, Eshkevari2022, Yang2024, Yue2024) and Lyft (Azagirre2024) is the strongest empirical base. On the pricing side, the OR/economics line has largely settled the foundational welfare and spatial-pricing questions (Besbes2021, Afeche2023, Castillo2019/2025), while DRL-based joint pricing and matching is represented by a single paper in this corpus (Zhang2023) — the bounded-out MFG-RL and DRL-pricing literatures listed in Section 11 are exactly where the bridge would come from. The LLM thread is conceptually the broadest but empirically the thinnest: foundation models for urban ST prediction (Yuan2024, Li2024) and one peer-reviewed LLM-as-reward-designer (Han2025) coexist with an LLM-as-controller preprint (Lyu2025) and one peer-reviewed joint matching+repositioning under fairness constraints (Sun2024).

## 5. Themes (Step 4 product) and MEAL paragraphs (Step 6 product)

This section presents six themes drawn from the matrices: two consensus, **one** controversy (downgraded from two in v2 — the corrected Theme 4 is now a coverage gap rather than a field-level controversy), and **three** gap themes (the third gap, formerly merged into Theme 4's framing, is broken out in v3 as Theme 4 itself). Each theme is one MEAL paragraph (Main idea, Evidence, Analysis, Link), written so that the grammatical subjects of sentences are concepts and findings, not author names. Every theme cites the specific rows in the matrices that support and (where applicable) contradict it. All headline numbers used in this section are verified against the source PDFs (Appendix A); no `(unverified)` numbers anchor any claim.

### 5.1 Theme 1 (consensus, with active contestation) — RL-proposed weights plus a combinatorial matcher is the dominant matching architecture in this corpus, but it is no longer uncontested

**Main idea.** The most common architectural template for matching is "RL learns edge weights on the driver–order bipartite graph; a combinatorial step (Hungarian, ILP, or Gale–Shapley) takes those weights and produces a feasible assignment" — but two recent papers (Yue2024, Lyu2025) explicitly contest the template, and the contestation is thin (one peer-reviewed paper on Didi data; one preprint), so the right framing is "dominant template, early contestation" rather than "won." **Evidence.** This template appears in every deployed system in the corpus — Didi's learning + planning approach (Xu2018), Didi's online state-value iteration (Eshkevari2022), Lyft's online matching RL (Azagirre2024) — as well as in the strongest non-deployed graph-MARL baselines (Wang2025; Hu2025, where the combinatorial step is an ILP with a learned posterior score). The bi-level AMoD line (Gammelli2021, Gammelli2023, Singhal2024, Tresca2026) follows the same template with an LP rather than a bipartite-matching CO solver. Two papers contest the template: Yue2024 ("D2SN") replaces the two-stage pipeline with a single end-to-end DRL model and reports verified 0.7–3.9% TDI gains on Didi data, and Lyu2025 has the LLM directly emit assignment+reposition actions with paper-claimed gains across fleet sizes (preprint, not peer reviewed). The TUM hybrid MARL line (Enders 2023: 5% avg / 17% peak; Hoppe 2024: 1.9% vs LRA / 3.5% avg vs greedy) is consistent with the template, and v3's verified numbers from those papers no longer carry an "unverified" caveat. **Analysis.** The persistence of the two-stage template is best explained by feasibility: the matching step has hard constraints (one-driver-per-order, geo-fenced batches) that the RL action space cannot easily encode, while the combinatorial solver guarantees feasibility regardless of how noisy or out-of-distribution the learned weights are. End-to-end approaches inherit the burden of feasibility through architectural tricks (autoregressive decoding, hold-actions). **Link.** A current research project in this space should default to the two-stage template unless it has a specific architectural reason to depart from it; if it does depart, it should report the same matching feasibility guarantees the two-stage template provides for free, and benchmark explicitly against Yue2024's verified 0.7–3.9% TDI as the bar to beat.

### 5.2 Theme 2 (consensus within the corpus reviewed) — graph encoders are the standard state representation for spatial dependence

**Main idea.** Within this corpus, the spatial state of a ride-hailing system is routinely encoded with graph neural networks (GNNs), and the choice of GNN variant — GCN, GAT, MPNN — is treated as a tunable rather than a load-bearing modeling decision. The corpus is not unbiased (the bounded-out ST-GNN forecasting literature is excluded), so the claim is scoped to the corpus rather than to the field as a whole. **Evidence.** The bi-level AMoD line uses graph convolutions (Gammelli2021), and its 2023 generalization explicitly studies MPNN/GCN/GAT variants and reports that graph-based encoders dominate MLP/CNN baselines via permutation invariance and locality (Gammelli2023). Graph attention is the most common encoder in current MARL: GAT-MF combines attention with mean field for >3,000 agents (Hao2023, with the caveat that the 42.7% headline is across heterogeneous benchmarks not all of which are ride-hailing); BMG-Q uses graph-attention DDQN over a localized bipartite match graph (Hu2025); CoopRide encodes city-scale grid relationships with a GNN (Wang2025). UrbanGPT and UniST treat the spatiotemporal graph as the substrate for foundation-model pretraining (Yuan2024, Li2024). The only papers in the corpus that do not use a GNN are the OR/economics pricing papers, which work in continuous space (Besbes2021, Afeche2023, Castillo2019/2025), and the pre-GNN-era MARL formulations (Lin2018, Li2019). **Analysis.** Two structural facts make GNNs an attractive default: cities are explicitly graph-structured (zones, road segments, charging stations) and ride-hailing decisions exhibit spatial locality at the right scale for message-passing depth of 1–3 hops. The remaining methodological choice — attention vs. fixed-weight aggregation — appears to matter less than whether some graph encoder is present at all, but this conclusion is sensitive to the corpus selection and may not survive expansion to the ST-GNN forecasting literature where attention vs. convolution debates are active. **Link.** A new ride-hailing controller can adopt graph attention (GAT) or message-passing (MPNN) as the default encoder without a methodological justification beyond "this is the corpus standard"; effort is better spent on the *coordination geometry* (Theme 3) than on the encoder choice.

### 5.3 Theme 3 (controversy) — agent definition: driver/vehicle vs. grid/region

**Main idea.** The corpus is split on whether the basic MARL agent should be a **driver/vehicle** or a **grid/region**, and the choice has cascading consequences for credit assignment, action space size, and how cooperation is modeled. **Evidence.** Driver-as-agent appears in early mean-field MARL (Li2019), the Lyft and Didi production deployments (Azagirre2024, Eshkevari2022), the TUM hybrid MARL line (Enders2023, Hoppe2024), the BMG-Q ride-pooling line (Hu2025), the constrained MFRL line (Jusup2025), and the LLM controllers (Lyu2025). Grid-as-agent appears in CoopRide (Wang2025), GAT-MF's grid-world variant (Hao2023), Yang2024's region cooperative game (the ODCMG formulation, v3 corrected), and the original contextual MARL (Lin2018). Production deployments lean driver-as-agent, while the strongest 2024–25 academic MARL benchmarks (Wang2025) use grid-as-agent. **Analysis.** The driver-as-agent formulation is closer to the operational reality (drivers are the decision-makers) and inherits cleaner credit assignment in the small-fleet regime, but the joint-action space grows with fleet size and forces mean-field or attention-weighted approximations. The grid-as-agent formulation collapses the action space (one action per cell) but loses driver-level personalization and creates a new credit-assignment problem because grid-level rewards do not directly attribute to driver-level decisions. Yang2024 makes the credit-assignment problem of grid-as-agent explicit (the ODCMG formulation is exactly the question of how a region's value affects others) and proposes goal-reaching collaboration as the answer. CoopRide partially closes the gap by giving each grid both within-grid and cross-grid actions, recovering some driver-level expressivity. **Link.** A research project should pick the agent definition by what its evaluation metric looks like: revenue/GMV-style metrics favor grid-as-agent (cleaner aggregation), while individual-driver metrics (fairness, income variance, acceptance rate) favor driver-as-agent.

### 5.4 Theme 4 (downgraded in v3 — coverage gap, not field controversy) — this corpus does not contain a DRL pricing paper that engages with OR/economics equilibrium results

**Main idea.** *This corpus contains exactly one DRL pricing paper (Zhang2023), and that paper does not engage with the strategic-driver, welfare-incidence, or equilibrium-anticipating results of the OR/economics pricing line in the corpus.* The right framing in v3 is therefore not "the field's DRL pricing is disconnected from OR/economics" — that is a field-level claim that one paper cannot support — but *"this corpus's coverage of DRL pricing is too thin to characterize the field, and the bounded-out MFG-RL literature (§11) is exactly where the bridge would be."* **Evidence.** The OR/economics track has shown that local myopic surge is suboptimal under strategic supply (Besbes2021), that admission control can be optimal under strategic drivers (Afeche2023), and — with the v2 direction correction and v3 citation hygiene flag — that surge pricing increases total welfare by 3.53% relative to a uniform-pricing counterfactual on Uber Houston data, with riders gaining 6.97% in surplus and drivers losing 1.97% (Castillo 2019 working paper / Econometrica 2025; v3 reads the working-paper version). The DRL pricing line is represented in this corpus solely by joint future-aware pricing + matching for ride-pooling (Zhang2023, v3 verified: average +6.4% revenue / −10.6% vehicle reduction; maxima +17% / −14%) on a NYC simulator, which does not model strategic drivers, does not characterize equilibrium, and does not benchmark against welfare-incidence findings. **Analysis.** A single DRL pricing paper cannot characterize the DRL pricing literature; the field-level controversy framing of v1 and v2 was overstated. The disconnect *as observed in this corpus* is partly a venue mismatch (Econometrica/MS vs. AAAI/KDD) and partly a methodological mismatch (closed-form fluid analysis vs. simulator-based DRL). However, the bounded-out MFG-RL literature (Guo et al.; Yang Yaodong's MFG-RL line; Saldi et al.) has been actively building this bridge for ~5 years, and the additional DRL-pricing seed citations in §11 (Mao et al.; Tang et al.'s pricing/incentive RL work at Didi) may already either contest or harden the framing once added. **Link.** A new pricing project should adopt the strategic-driver assumption from the OR/economics literature as a baseline and benchmark its DRL policy against the comparative-static predictions of Besbes 2021 and the welfare decomposition of Castillo 2019/2025 (with Houston/Uber scoping). It should also engage explicitly with the MFG-RL bridge literature rather than treating ML and OR as disconnected. *The specific scoped research question that should anchor such a project is described in Gap A0 (§7.1)*, which v3 promotes from §8 to a load-bearing position.

### 5.5 Theme 5 (gap) — true three-way joint optimization of matching + pricing + rebalancing

**Main idea.** Joint optimization is the visibly emerging frontier, but **no paper in the corpus addresses all three operational levers (matching + pricing + rebalancing) simultaneously with peer-reviewed evidence**. **Evidence.** Pairwise joint optimization exists: matching + repositioning under fairness (Sun2024 JDRCL); matching + repositioning by LLM agent (Lyu2025); pricing + matching for ride-pooling via ADP (Zhang2023, v3 verified). The Pavone-lab AMoD line couples matching and rebalancing through bi-level optimization (Gammelli2021, Singhal2024, Tresca2026 with v3-corrected location-specific gap-from-oracle numbers) but treats pricing as exogenous. The MARL lines (Wang2025, Hao2023, Hu2025) treat pricing as exogenous as well. **Analysis.** Two reasons explain the gap. First, the matching/rebalancing levers act on second-to-minute timescales while pricing acts on minute-to-longer timescales; coupling them in a single Markov decision process inflates the state and action spaces and makes off-policy learning unstable. Second, pricing changes demand, which in turn changes the post-matching idle distribution that rebalancing acts on; a three-way joint policy must internalize that feedback, which the equilibrium literature handles via fluid models but DRL has only partially absorbed. JDRCL's max-min fairness convergence guarantee shows that constrained multi-objective MGs can be analyzed in the pairwise case; extending such analysis to three-way is the unsolved technical step. **Link.** A research project that aims to fill this gap should (a) work in the pairwise matching+rebalancing setting first, with a max-min fairness or similar constraint, before adding pricing; (b) borrow the strategic-driver fluid abstraction from Besbes2021/Afeche2023 as the third decision module; and (c) evaluate the joint policy against the welfare incidence of surge documented in Castillo 2019/2025 to test whether three-way joint optimization changes the distributional answer.

### 5.6 Theme 6 (gap, with the v2 counter-reason promoted into the topic sentence) — LLM role in ride-hailing is unstandardized **but the corpus is also too small to support standardization**

**Main idea.** The LLM/foundation-model thread is the most rapidly growing line in the corpus, but the **role** an LLM plays in the pipeline (encoder, forecaster, reward designer, planner, simulator) is not yet standardized — and with only four LLM/FM papers in the corpus, the lack of standardization is partly a sample-size artifact rather than a deeply structural problem. The premature-consolidation risk that the v2 gap analysis identified (a "shared benchmark" with N=4 risks freezing out role choices that have not yet been tried — LLM as simulator, pricing engine, fairness auditor) is now part of the theme statement itself rather than only appearing in the gap analysis. **Evidence.** Within the corpus, the LLM is a forecaster in UrbanGPT and UniST (Yuan2024, Li2024), a reward designer in GARLIC (Han2025; v3 reports the arithmetically reconciled 2.48 pp empty-load reduction against the best baseline), and a direct planner in LLM-ODDR (Lyu2025). The first two are pretrained ST foundation models with peer-reviewed cross-city zero/few-shot generalization (UniST 10.1% over no-pretrain; UrbanGPT ~28% over generalization baselines). The third is peer-reviewed at AAAI but uses GPT only to shape rewards, not to act. The fourth (Lyu2025) is the only paper that gives the LLM the controller role and reports +5.21–48.87% GMV across fleet sizes, but it is an unreviewed preprint with 1–5 s/decision inference latency. **Analysis.** The role mismatch has two practical consequences. First, claims about "LLM for ride-hailing" cannot be compared head-to-head because the LLMs are in different positions in the pipeline; a 28% gain as encoder is not commensurable with a 2.48 pp empty-load reduction as reward designer. Second, no benchmark in the corpus standardizes city, fleet size, and metric the way the AMoD line did with the macroscopic 16-station simulator (Gammelli2021); the LLM line is therefore at risk of pattern over-fitting on case studies. **However, the right pre-benchmark step is probably a role-comparison study (run the same problem with the LLM in three roles and report) rather than a frozen benchmark, exactly because the role taxonomy itself is not yet stable.** **Link.** A new project should pick a single LLM role (planner, reward designer, or forecaster) and benchmark against a non-LLM baseline of the same operational class; if the project uses an LLM as a controller, latency and grounding (hallucinated assignments) should be measured explicitly. A useful contribution to the field would be a *role-comparison* study, not yet a frozen benchmark.

## 6. Where the synthesis matrix exposed surprises (corpus-specific)

Three patterns surfaced in the matrices that were not visible in the per-paper schema entries.

A first surprise is that **no paper in the corpus uses the rider as the agent**, despite riders being treated as strategic in the OR/economics track (Castillo 2019/2025 estimates rider price-elasticity from data; Besbes 2021 carries strategic riders as a structural feature). The MARL formulations universally agentize either drivers or grids, and the LLM controller (Lyu2025) agentizes the LLM-controlled vehicle. This is a corpus-specific absence that is invisible in the per-paper schema — every paper has a defensible agent definition for its purpose — but visible only in Matrix 2's lateral view. The absence is consequential for fairness and welfare framings: rider-side metrics (waiting time, surge incidence, allocation equity) are evaluated post-hoc rather than directly optimized. This is the most insightful corpus-specific finding in the review and is given more weight in v3 than in v2.

A second surprise is that **the bi-level paradigm is so dominant in AMoD that the controversy is no longer "RL vs OR" but "which inner problem"**. Gammelli's lab uses an LP for network-flow rebalancing; the TUM lab uses bipartite matching; both share the outer GNN-RL. The methodological agreement is much stronger than a non-specialist would guess from the venue diversity (CDC, ICML, ECC, TCNS, L4DC). Whether this convergence survives the addition of MFG-RL to the corpus (Section 11) is an open question.

A third surprise is that **fairness has only just entered the corpus as a first-class objective**. JDRCL (Sun2024) gives fairness a max-min formulation with a convergence guarantee; Jusup2025 gives accessibility the role of a hard constraint; Lyu2025 includes a fairness-aware dispatching subroutine. Outside these three, fairness in ride-hailing ML is post-hoc or absent. This is a recognizable pattern in ML subfields where a constraint becomes first-class only after the core control problem stabilizes; the literature is at the start of that transition.

### 6.5 Methodological caveats common across the corpus (new in v3)

Per-paper hedges are necessary but insufficient: several methodological issues recur across the corpus and are best discussed once, here, rather than scattered across schema entries.

**Switchback identification under spatial spillovers.** Azagirre 2024 (Lyft) reports its $30M/yr incremental-revenue claim from production switchback experiments. Switchbacks alternate treatment across time blocks within a single market and assume that within-block effects are local and additive across blocks. Both assumptions are stressed in ride-hailing settings: spatial spillovers (a treated block's matching outcomes change the idle distribution that the next block inherits) and time-varying treatment effects (peak-hour treatment is not the same intervention as off-peak treatment) bias estimators in ways that the recent design-of-experiments literature has documented (Bojinov & Shephard on adaptive experimentation; Glynn–Johari–Rasouli on switchback identification under interference). The Lyft headline number should therefore be read as a bounded estimate with non-trivial residual identification risk, not as a clean causal estimate.

**Off-policy evaluation variance.** Yang 2024 explicitly flags OPE variance at the per-paper level (±7–10pp on per-horizon estimates). Across the corpus, OPE drives several headline numbers (Yang 2024 ~5–6%; Yue 2024 0.7–3.9% TDI). The corpus-wide pattern is that **a non-trivial share of "RL works at scale" claims rest on OPE rather than on prospective deployment**, and OPE estimators are known to be high-variance under distribution shift. Readers should weight prospective-deployment numbers (Xu 2018, Eshkevari 2022, Azagirre 2024) more heavily than OPE numbers when assessing the literature's evidentiary base.

**Publication bias in deployed-RL reports.** Production deployments that fail to ship or are rolled back are not published; the corpus's deployed-system papers (Xu 2018, Eshkevari 2022, Azagirre 2024) are without exception their respective platforms' own success reports. The selection bias is structural — there is no natural way for negative deployed-RL results to enter the literature — but it should temper any reading of "RL works at production scale" that draws on the small set of platform-authored success reports as evidence of generality.

**Concurrent-improvement confounding.** Xu 2018 explicitly notes that the ~10% completion-rate gain it cites came from a *driver-select→platform-assign architectural change*, not from RL itself; the RL-attributable share of the headline is the smaller 0.5–5% revenue figure. This is a corpus-wide concern: production deployments ship many things at once, and percentage gains attributed to "the deployed RL system" often confound RL with concurrent platform improvements. The 2018 Xu paper is unusually clear about this; later deployed-system papers in the corpus are less clear, and the headline percentages should be read with this caveat in mind.

These four caveats apply most directly to the deployed-RL line (Thread A) but also affect the bi-level AMoD line's claim of generalization (Tresca 2026's macro→meso zero-shot gap is large precisely because cross-fidelity transfer is hard) and the LLM line's reported gains (which are case-study-style and not yet evaluated under switchback or OPE scrutiny).

## 7. Gap analysis (Step 5 product)

Four candidate gaps are evaluated below against the importance/reasonableness/feasibility criteria. v3 promotes a new **Gap A0** — *benchmark a DRL three-way controller against Castillo's welfare decomposition on the Houston market* — from v2's §8 into §7 as the load-bearing, scoped, falsifiable research question (the senior reviewer explicitly identified this as the strongest scoped gap and recommended its promotion). v3 retains v2's Gap A (three-way joint optimization with strategic-agent guarantees), Gap B (LLM role taxonomy), and Gap C (cross-city transfer / sim-to-real); each retains its v2 counter-reason and verdict.

### 7.1 Gap A0 (load-bearing, v3 promoted) — benchmark a DRL three-way controller against Castillo's welfare decomposition on the Houston market

*Importance.* This is the single specific research question that every preceding theme of the review converges on: Theme 4's coverage gap identifies the absent DRL/equilibrium bridge, Theme 5's three-way gap names the technical scope of a controller that would close it, and §6.5's methodological caveats specify how the benchmark must be defended. A successful DRL three-way controller benchmarked on Castillo 2019/2025's Houston data with the welfare decomposition (+3.53% total welfare; +6.97% rider; −1.97% driver) as the *target metric structure* — not just total revenue — would settle whether DRL can match, exceed, or distort the welfare incidence that the OR equilibrium model produced. The importance is therefore not generic ("this has implications for…"); it is the specific empirical-comparison move that the corpus has made possible.

*Reasonableness.* Castillo 2019/2025 published the welfare decomposition that the comparison requires; Sun 2024 (JDRCL) showed that constrained multi-objective MARL is convergent in the pairwise case; Zhang 2023 showed that joint pricing+matching DRL is feasible on a NYC simulator (with v3-verified average +6.4% revenue and −10.6% vehicles). The field cares about welfare incidence (the Castillo paper exists in Econometrica because it answers a load-bearing economics question), and DRL's ability to match welfare-side targets — not just platform-side targets — is exactly what the OR/economics critique of v2 was about.

*Feasibility.* Houston Uber data are not public, but a comparable open dataset (e.g., a public Houston taxi dataset, NYC taxi data with a Houston-calibrated demand model, or a synthetic Houston model from the Castillo paper's structural estimates) is feasible for a 1–2 year project. The metric structure (total welfare, rider surplus, driver surplus) is decomposable from a DRL controller's simulator outputs given a calibrated demand-elasticity model. The pairwise (matching+repositioning) controller (Sun 2024 / Lyu 2025) is a feasible starting point; pricing as the third lever can be added on a coarser timescale to manage state-space inflation.

*Counter-reason.* The Castillo 2019/2025 welfare decomposition is identified on Uber Houston data with reduced-form variation; a DRL controller trained on a different data-generating process (NYC taxi simulator, synthetic Houston) will produce welfare numbers that are not directly comparable to Castillo's even if they report the same structural decomposition. The benchmark therefore tests *whether the DRL controller's welfare structure aligns with the equilibrium-model's structure*, not whether the DRL controller produces the same numbers. This is still useful — alignment vs. divergence is an informative result either way — but the benchmark's strength is structural rather than numerical.

*Verdict (v3).* Real, scoped, falsifiable gap. This is the gap a thesis chapter or pilot study should anchor on. Promoting it here from v2's §8 (where it appeared as one bullet of operational implications) is the v3 fix the senior reviewer flagged.

### 7.2 Gap A — three-way joint optimization with strategic-agent guarantees

*Importance.* Filling this gap meaningfully changes the answer to the core platform-control question, because the equilibrium literature has already shown that pricing and rebalancing are coupled through strategic supply (Besbes2021, Afeche2023). A controller that optimizes only matching+rebalancing while taking pricing as exogenous misses the equilibrium feedback that determines welfare incidence (Castillo 2019/2025).

*Reasonableness.* The gap connects to two questions the field cares about: Castillo 2019/2025's distributional result shows that surge has heterogeneous welfare incidence on Uber Houston data, and DRL pricing in this corpus has not engaged with that result; JDRCL's convergence guarantee for two-way joint matching+repositioning under max-min fairness shows that three-way extension is a recognizable technical question in the same lineage.

*Feasibility.* Two-way joint matching+repositioning has been done (JDRCL, Lyu2025); three-way joint matching+repositioning+pricing requires either (a) coupling a fluid-model pricing module with a DRL matching+repositioning module, or (b) a single MARL formulation with prices as agent actions. (a) is feasible with current methods; (b) is harder but not impossible — the action space inflation is manageable if pricing acts on a coarser timescale.

*Counter-reason* (carried from v2). The OR/economics fluid-model line has, in fact, solved versions of three-way joint optimization analytically (Besbes 2021's network-anticipating pricing implicitly couples spatial pricing with the supply repositioning it induces; Afeche 2023 couples admission control with relocation incentives). The right framing of Gap A may therefore be "the ML literature should adopt the equilibrium framing rather than reinvent three-way coupling in MDPs," not "three-way joint optimization has not been done."

*Verdict (v3).* Real gap on the *implementation-as-DRL* axis (no peer-reviewed three-way DRL paper exists in this corpus); partly closed on the *analytical solvability* axis (OR equilibrium analysis has solved adjacent versions). Gap A0 (§7.1) is the scoped specialization of this gap that the v3 review is anchoring on.

### 7.3 Gap B — LLM role taxonomy and shared benchmark

*Importance.* The LLM line is growing fast and the role mismatch (encoder, forecaster, reward designer, planner) makes head-to-head comparison impossible. A standardized comparison is therefore high-importance for any near-term consolidation of the line.

*Reasonableness.* The AMoD subfield's bi-level GNN-RL line consolidated rapidly once Gammelli2021's macroscopic 16-station benchmark appeared; the LLM line is at the same pre-benchmark stage and would benefit from a similar unifying comparison.

*Feasibility.* A role-comparison study requires (a) a shared simulator/data — most LLM ride-hailing papers already use Manhattan or NYC taxi data, so a NYC-taxi standard is plausible; (b) a shared metric — empty-load rate or GMV, both already widely reported; (c) a shared role-axis — encoder/forecaster/reward-designer/planner. Building a study that fixes (a) and (b) and varies (c) is feasible within a single research project.

*Counter-reason* (carried from v2, now also embedded in Theme 6's topic sentence). The LLM line in this corpus is only four papers (Yuan2024, Li2024, Han2025, Lyu2025); a "shared benchmark" with such a small N risks overfitting the benchmark to the four extant role choices and freezing out roles that have not yet been tried (LLM as simulator, LLM as pricing engine, LLM as fairness auditor). The pre-benchmark stage may be the right stage; consolidation may be premature.

*Verdict (v3).* Real gap, but feasibility-constrained: a benchmark designed too early may calcify the role taxonomy. The best near-term project is a *role-comparison study* (run the same problem with the LLM in three roles and report) rather than a frozen benchmark.

### 7.4 Gap C — cross-city transfer and sim-to-real for production deployment

*Importance.* Production deployments (Xu2018, Eshkevari2022, Azagirre2024) are tied to single platforms. Cross-city transfer determines whether the academic methods (Wang2025, Hu2025, Tresca2026) translate to the next platform without per-city retraining. The importance follows from the asymmetry: there are far more cities than papers.

*Reasonableness.* Cross-city transfer is explicitly demonstrated for the bi-level AMoD line (Gammelli2021 zero-shot Chengdu↔NYC; Tresca2026 meta-RL ≈ +61% gain on the Brooklyn special-event scenario [v3 corrected]). Foundation models in the LLM line claim cross-city zero-shot for forecasting (UniST: MAE 26.84 cross-city; UrbanGPT: ~28% over generalization baselines). The gap is whether these forecasting/rebalancing transfers extend to **deployed matching policies** at production scale, where the production lineage has not reported transfer numbers.

*Feasibility.* Two paths: (a) extend Tresca2026's meta-RL across truly heterogeneous cities (not just NYC↔Brooklyn↔Shenzhen); (b) use a foundation-model encoder (UniST/UrbanGPT) as the state representation for a matching policy and test whether the foundation-model's cross-city generalization transfers to the policy. Both are within a 1–2 year project.

*Counter-reason* (carried from v2). Cross-city transfer at the *forecasting* layer is likely to be much easier than at the *matching-policy* layer, because matching policies are tightly coupled to platform-specific operational constraints (driver pool composition, regulatory constraints, surge rules, dispatch latency budgets) that are not captured by ST data alone. A foundation-model encoder may transfer cleanly while the policy on top still requires full per-city retraining.

*Verdict (v3).* Real gap on the *full-pipeline transfer* axis; the *encoder-only transfer* version of the gap is already partly closed by UniST and UrbanGPT.

A fifth candidate gap — "fairness as a hard constraint" — partially fails the *reasonableness* test because the field is already moving in this direction (JDRCL, Jusup2025, Lyu2025). It is more accurate to call fairness an emerging consensus rather than a gap. A sixth candidate — "cross-city / population not yet studied" — fails the *importance* test as written; "population X has not been studied" is the bad-gap framing flagged by the guide.

## 8. Operational implications for a research project entering this field

The synthesis above implies five concrete defaults for a new research project in this space; the load-bearing research question is in §7.1.

A project should **default to the two-stage RL+combinatorial template** for matching unless it has an architectural reason to depart from it; if it does, it must show how feasibility is preserved (Theme 1). The bar to beat for end-to-end alternatives is Yue2024's verified 0.7–3.9% TDI on Didi data.

A project should **default to a graph encoder (GAT or MPNN)** for spatial state, and spend its modeling budget on the coordination geometry (driver-as-agent vs grid-as-agent) rather than the encoder choice (Themes 2 and 3). The encoder-default conclusion is scoped to the corpus reviewed and may not survive expansion to the ST-GNN forecasting literature.

A project working on dispatching at scale should **pre-decide** between driver-as-agent (cleaner driver-level metrics, harder credit assignment) and grid-as-agent (cleaner aggregate metrics, harder driver attribution) based on the metric of interest (Theme 3).

A project working on pricing should **adopt strategic agents** as a structural assumption and benchmark against the comparative-static predictions of Besbes2021 and the welfare decomposition of Castillo 2019/2025 (with corrected direction, explicit Houston/Uber scoping, and the citation-hygiene flag from §0); it should also engage with the bounded-out MFG-RL literature as the bridge between OR equilibrium and DRL control (Section 11), not treat the two as disconnected (Theme 4, v3 downgraded). The strategic-driver assumption needs empirical backing from the bounded-out driver-labor literature (Hall–Horton–Knoepfle; see §11), without which the recommendation is methodologically circular.

A project working on LLMs in ride-hailing should **pick one LLM role and one non-LLM baseline of the same operational class**, and report inference latency and grounding behavior; "LLM is the controller" with a 1–5 s decision latency is not a deployment-ready claim (Theme 6). A *role-comparison study* (one LLM, three roles, one problem) is the right pre-benchmark contribution.

Production-deployment claims should be evaluated against the §6.5 caveats (switchback identification, OPE variance, publication bias, concurrent-improvement confounding) rather than taken at face value; the headline percentages in this corpus are bounded estimates, not clean causal estimates.

## 11. v4 expansion plan: adjacent literatures the next-revision corpus should add

Section 1.1 bounded out five adjacent literatures with defenses. The list below names seed citations the v4 corpus should add. Inclusion of these papers will likely (i) soften or rewrite Theme 4 (because MFG-RL is the bridge and additional DRL-pricing papers may either contest or harden the framing), (ii) expand Theme 1's evidence base for ride-pooling, and (iii) strengthen the strategic-agent baseline that Section 8 recommends.

1. **Mechanism-design / IC spatial pricing**: Ma, Fang, Parkes, "Spatio-temporal pricing for ride-sharing platforms" (EC 2019 / OR follow-on); related work on auction-style ride-hailing pricing.
2. **Mean-field-game-aware RL**: Guo, Hu, Xu, Zhang on MFG-RL theoretical foundations (2019); Cui & Koeppl on approximately-stationary MFG-RL (2021); Yang Yaodong's MFG-RL line; Saldi et al. on equilibrium-aware learning. **This is the literature whose absence makes Theme 4's v1/v2 framing overstated; v4 should treat MFG-RL as a sixth thread.**
3. **Additional DRL pricing / surge / incentive RL** (new in v3): Mao et al. on dynamic-pricing RL; Tang et al.'s Didi-side work on pricing/incentive RL; Liu, Wu et al. on DRL surge. **If even two more DRL pricing papers exist with strategic-driver assumptions, Theme 4's v3 framing collapses further (from "coverage gap in this corpus" to "this is no longer a controversy at all"); if they do not, the framing hardens into a real disconnect.** This is the highest-priority single addition for v4.
4. **Tang et al.'s Didi MARL line** (KDD 2019, "A Deep Value-network Based Approach for Multi-Driver Order Dispatching") — a Thread A foundational citation that bridges Xu 2018 to Eshkevari 2022. The current review jumps from Xu 2018 to Eshkevari 2022 with no intermediate; v4 should cite this paper.
5. **Ride-pooling beyond BMG-Q**: Alonso-Mora et al. "On-demand high-capacity ride-sharing via dynamic trip-vehicle assignment" (PNAS 2017); descendants on capacity-constrained matching and shared-ride pricing. Without this, the Hu 2025 BMG-Q comparison is unmoored on the OR side.
6. **Driver labor economics**: Hall, Horton, Knoepfle, "Pricing in ride-sharing platforms: Driver supply"; Cook et al., "The gender earnings gap in the gig economy" (RES 2021). Provides empirical grounding for the strategic-driver assumption that §8 recommends; without it, the recommendation is methodologically circular.
7. **ST-GNN demand forecasting**: Li et al. "Diffusion Convolutional Recurrent Neural Network" (DCRNN, ICLR 2018); Wu et al. "Graph WaveNet" (IJCAI 2019); Yu et al. "STGCN" (IJCAI 2018). Provides the forecasting backbone that feeds into Theme 2's GNN-encoder default.

Adding 6–10 papers from this list would bring the corpus to 33–37, comfortably within the guide's 30–50 target. The single highest-value addition is the MFG-RL line (item 2) plus 2–3 additional DRL-pricing papers (item 3); together these are the additions that determine whether Theme 4 needs to be rewritten as "no controversy" (if the bridge literature is well-developed) or hardened as "real corpus-wide disconnect" (if the bridge literature is also thin).
