# Reviewer Report — *Literature review: deep RL, GNNs, and LLMs for ride-hailing matching, pricing, and rebalancing (2018–2026)*

## 1. Overall assessment

The review is competent in form — five well-axed synthesis matrices, six themes properly partitioned across consensus / controversy / gap, MEAL discipline largely respected, and a self-aware Section 0 admitting an earlier pass had fabricated numbers. But form alone does not earn it publication-grade status. Spot-checking the source PDFs surfaces at least one directional inversion of a flagship empirical result (Castillo 2025) and one number that does not appear in the table the review attributes it to (Yang 2024). The corpus is also narrow in ways the review does not own — 27 papers (below the guide's own 30–50 target), heavy Didi/Lyft/NYC concentration, and several adjacent literatures (mechanism-design pricing, ride-pooling beyond BMG-Q, driver labor economics, mean-field game equilibrium-aware RL) absent without acknowledgement. As a thesis chapter draft this needs **major revision** before it would survive a committee; as a journal submission it would not pass first-round desk review with the Castillo error in place.

## 2. Major issues, ordered by severity

**M1. Directional inversion of the Castillo (2025) result.** Section 2.4's schema entry reads: *"Eliminating surge → +3.53% gross-revenue welfare aggregate; riders +6.97%, drivers −1.97%."* The paper says the opposite. Its abstract states surge pricing **increases** total welfare by 3.53% **relative to uniform pricing**; rider surplus goes up 6.97% **with surge** versus without; drivers lose 1.97% **with surge**. So eliminating surge would *hurt* riders and *reduce* aggregate welfare, not improve it. Theme 4's prose (Section 5.4) gets the sign right, but the schema entry, which is the source-of-truth artifact the matrices are meant to be populated from, is wrong. This is exactly the failure mode the guide warns about — fluent prose laundering an upstream extraction error — and it is load-bearing for the entire pricing-track argument. Fix the schema and re-derive Matrix 4 and Theme 4 from the correct sign.

**M2. Yang (2024) numbers misattributed to a table that does not contain them.** The schema cites "0.24–1.43% IORR, 0.25–1.75% IGMV (city B OPE table)." On inspection of the paper's Table 1 City B OPE, the GRC method shows roughly 5–6% IORR/IGMV gains, not sub-2%. The 0.24–1.43% range likely comes from a different table (City A or simulation), and the review's "sub-2%" framing — which then anchors Theme 1's claim that production-grade RL gains are small — is sourced from the wrong cell. This is a citation-load error: the directional argument may still hold, but the empirical anchor is incorrect and must be re-extracted.

**M3. Two load-bearing numbers carry "(unverified)" labels and are still used in Themes.** Section 0 honestly admits that Enders (2023) "5–17% profit," Hoppe (2024) "2–6% performance," and Zhang (2023) "+17% revenue, −14% vehicles" were not re-verified. Yet Theme 1 cites the Enders number to define a seminal architectural template, and Theme 4 leans on Zhang (2023) as the entire DRL pricing exemplar in the controversy paragraph. A peer-reviewed literature review cannot use "unverified" as an escape valve for claims that anchor its argument. Either verify them and remove the label, or replace them with verified numbers from elsewhere in the corpus.

**M4. Corpus selection is not defended; coverage gaps are not flagged.** The review treats the 27-paper corpus as a given, sourced from `ride-share-deep-research.md`. The guide's Step 1 asks for explicit search strategy, two-database cross-checks, and seed-paper expansion; Section 10 ticks the "two databases" box but the document never describes the search itself. More substantively, several literatures that any senior reviewer in this area would expect to see are absent:

   - **Ride-pooling / shared rides** beyond Hu (2025) BMG-Q — the Alonso-Mora et al. PNAS 2017 framework and its descendants are canonical for capacity-constrained matching and are not represented.
   - **Mechanism-design / incentive-compatible spatial pricing** — Ma, Fang, Parkes and follow-on work on auction-style and IC pricing for ride-hailing — entirely missing despite being directly germane to Theme 4.
   - **Equilibrium-aware mean-field-game RL** — work from the Yang Yaodong / Pavone / Saldi lines on MFG-RL would be the natural bridge between the OR equilibrium track (Besbes, Afeche) and the DRL track (Zhang 2023) that Theme 4 declares disconnected. Its absence weakens the controversy claim: the literature *is* trying to bridge, the review just hasn't found those papers.
   - **Driver labor economics** (Hall, Horton, Knoepfle on Uber; Cook et al. on the gender earnings gap) — the strategic-driver assumption that Theme 4 wants DRL to adopt is empirically grounded in this literature, which is uncited.
   - **Demand-forecasting backbone** as distinct from control — the review collapses forecasting into UniST/UrbanGPT but ignores the ST-GNN forecasting literature (DCRNN, Graph WaveNet, STGCN) that feeds into the GNN-encoder choice it endorses in Theme 2.

   I am confident about the first three of these as suspected omissions; the last two I am moderately confident about. Either way, the review should declare its inclusion/exclusion criteria and explicitly state which adjacent literatures it has chosen to bound out, and why.

**M5. Geographic and platform concentration is not problematized.** Of the deployed/empirical work, Didi accounts for five papers (Xu, Eshkevari, Yang, Yue, Zhang), Lyft for one, and the remaining empirical work is NYC-taxi-derived. Bolt / FreeNow (EU), Ola (India), Grab / Gojek (Southeast Asia), and DiDi-Brazil deployments are absent. The Castillo result is single-city (Houston). Theme 4's distributional welfare claim is treated as if it generalized, but the review never scopes it to Houston or notes that the +6.97% rider-surplus number is one estimate from one structural model on one market.

**M6. Section 7's gap analysis clears all three candidates uncritically.** Each of Gap A (three-way joint), Gap B (LLM benchmark), Gap C (cross-city transfer) gets "Verdict: Real gap, all three criteria pass." A senior reviewer reading three positive verdicts in a row should be suspicious that the criteria are not biting. Specifically, Gap A risks the "no one has done it" framing the guide warns against — the *technical* importance of unifying the three controls in a single MDP is asserted but not argued against the alternative that the OR/economics fluid-model line *has* solved versions of three-way coupling and the ML community simply has not absorbed those results. The right framing of Gap A is probably "the ML literature should adopt the equilibrium framing," not "no one has done three-way." Re-examine each gap with at least one reason it might *not* be a real gap, and let the verdict turn on which reason is stronger.

**M7. "Seminal" is used as a stance label without consistent justification.** Lin (2018), Li (2019), Enders (2023), Gammelli (2021), and Besbes (2021) are all called "seminal." For some this is defensible (Gammelli 2021 launches the bi-level GNN-RL line; Besbes 2021 is foundational for spatial pricing). For others (Enders 2023 is two years old) the label is premature. Either define the criterion (citation count threshold, paradigm-defining role, etc.) or downgrade to "early" / "first-of-kind."

## 3. Minor issues

The corpus is 27 papers, against the guide's own target of 30–50; Section 10 nonetheless ticks this box. Acknowledge the shortfall or add seed papers from the missing literatures listed above. Tag dictionary in Section 2.6 lists 18 reconciled tags but actually contains roughly 50 distinct strings; the count should be corrected. Section 2.5's note that "Sun2024 (JDRCL)" appears in the LLM/foundation-model thread "for joint-optimization comparability" is honest but creates a categorical inconsistency — Sun (2024) is not an LLM/foundation-model paper and should be threaded under MARL/joint-optimization with a cross-reference, not placed where it disrupts Matrix 5. Several venue claims should be spot-verified: "Sun2024 — TKDE 2024 (KDD'22 ext.)" and "Tresca2026 — TCNS 2026 (in press)" both warrant a check against the journals' tables of contents (I did not verify these in this review). Section 6's "Surprises" is thin — the deployed-vs-academic effect-size gap is well-documented in the wider RL literature and is not specific to this corpus; reframe or drop. Theme 2's claim that "graph attention is the default" is consistent with the corpus but the corpus is a biased sample; soften to "within the corpus reviewed." Theme 1's "the architectural template that has won the matching subproblem" is overstated given Yue (2024) and Lyu (2025) explicitly contest it; the prose acknowledges them but the topic sentence does not. Citations to Hao (2023) repeatedly mix a general "performance" gain (42.7%) with a ride-hailing-specific claim — the paper's headline is across heterogeneous benchmarks, and the review should not present 42.7% as a ride-hailing dispatch number without the qualification that already appears in the limitations cell.

## 4. Specific suggestions for revision, in priority order

Fix the Castillo (2025) directional error in Section 2.4 first. Re-extract the Yang (2024) IORR/IGMV figures from the correct table cell and check whether Theme 1's "production gains are small" claim survives. Verify or replace the three "(unverified)" headline numbers (Enders, Hoppe, Zhang 2023) — load-bearing claims in a literature review should not carry that label at submission. Add a one-paragraph "search strategy and inclusion criteria" subsection at the start of Section 2 that names the databases queried, the date window, the keyword set, and the explicit exclusions (mechanism-design pricing, driver labor economics, ride-pooling beyond a single paper, etc.), with a defense of each exclusion or a commitment to add the literature. Add at least one paper each from mechanism-design pricing, MFG-RL, and the Alonso-Mora ride-pooling line; if added, Theme 4's "DRL pricing is not in dialogue with OR/economics" claim will need to be softened, because MFG-RL is exactly that dialogue. Rewrite Section 7 so that each gap is challenged with a counter-reason and the verdict turns on which side of the argument is stronger; passing all three uniformly is not a pass, it is a tell. Scope Theme 4's distributional welfare claim explicitly to Houston / Uber / Castillo's identification strategy, and note that cross-market generalization is unverified. Tighten the "seminal" usage. Re-count the tag dictionary and reconcile with the 18-tag claim. Strengthen Section 6 by removing the deployed-vs-academic gap (well known) and replacing it with a corpus-specific surprise — for example, the absence of any paper that uses the rider as the agent is a genuine and corpus-specific observation worth surfacing.

## 5. What the review does well, briefly

The honest source-attribution note in Section 0 is the single best thing in the document — most AI-generated reviews would have buried the earlier fabrications. The five matrices each have a single, defensible comparison axis, and the `comparability_notes` rows show real lateral thinking, not just paper-by-paper indexing. MEAL discipline holds in most paragraphs; the summary-mode test in Section 10 is a real check, not theatre. Theme 3 (driver-as-agent vs grid-as-agent) is a substantive controversy claim that the matrices actually evidence, and the analysis sentence (action-space-vs-credit-assignment trade-off) is a real mechanism, not a hedge. Section 8's operational-implications list translates the synthesis into design defaults — most literature reviews do not do this, and it is the right move. Preserve all of these on revision; the issues above are mostly extraction errors and coverage gaps, not structural failures of the synthesis.
