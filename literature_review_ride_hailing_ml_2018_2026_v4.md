## 1. The question this review is answering

Ride-hailing platforms make three operational decisions on a spatiotemporal graph of a city: **whom to assign to whom (matching)**, **what to charge (pricing)**, and **where idle drivers should go (rebalancing)**. These decisions are coupled — pricing changes demand, demand shapes matching feasibility, and matching outcomes determine the post-trip idle distribution that rebalancing then has to correct. The question this review is answering is: *what does the 2018–2026 ML and OR literature on these three decisions look like, separately and jointly, and where are the bridges and gaps between them?*

The five threads from `ride-share-deep-research.md` are kept as a coverage device but are not used as the organizing principle of the prose, which is theme-first by design.

### 1.1 Search strategy, inclusion criteria, and bounded-out adjacent literatures

**Databases and time window.** The corpus was assembled by (a) seeding from `ride-share-deep-research.md`, an in-house deep-research compilation; (b) cross-checking against arXiv (2018–April 2026), ACM Digital Library (KDD, CIKM, WWW, AAAI), PMLR (ICML, L4DC), IEEE Xplore (CDC, ECC, T-ITS, TCNS), INFORMS journals (Management Science, MSOM, Journal on Applied Analytics), and Econometrica (Wiley). The default time window is 2018–2026, with explicit carve-outs for landmark older works that seed reviews cite (e.g., Lin 2018, Xu 2018).

**Keyword set.** "Ride-hailing" OR "ride-sourcing" OR "AMoD" OR "autonomous mobility on demand" OR "robo-taxi" — combined with — "deep reinforcement learning" OR "MARL" OR "multi-agent" OR "graph neural network" OR "GNN" OR "foundation model" OR "LLM" OR "large language model" — combined with — "matching" OR "dispatching" OR "rebalancing" OR "repositioning" OR "fleet management" OR "pricing" OR "surge".

**Inclusion criteria.** Peer-reviewed papers in the venues above plus arXiv preprints with claim-level evidence (a clearly stated method, a defined dataset, and a numeric result). Position papers and tutorials were excluded. Where multiple versions of a paper exist (preprint + journal), the most recent peer-reviewed version that could be verified against a PDF is cited; where the published version's numbers were not separately re-checked, this is flagged inline.

**Corpus size and counting convention.** **26 unique papers**, **counted as 27 in threadwise listings under the Sun 2024 cross-listing convention** (Sun 2024 / JDRCL is the only paper appearing in two threads — primary placement Thread B for its constrained-MARL methodology, cross-referenced from Thread E for joint-optimization comparability). All theme-level claims and matrices reference Sun 2024 once on the substantive level; the count discrepancy is purely bibliographic.

The corpus is **below the guide's 30–50 target**, and the shortfall is the single largest unresolved scope critique of v4. v4 has not added new papers relative to v3 — the prof-flagged corpus expansion has been deferred to v5 because adding new sources requires verifying their PDFs and numeric extractions to the same standard as the existing entries, which a cosmetic-revision pass cannot accomplish. Section 11 names 6–10 specific seed citations for v5; until expansion lands, theme-level claims about "the field" are scoped to the corpus reviewed, and three theme paragraphs (Themes 4, 6, and the controversy/gap framings of each) are downgraded to corpus-coverage observations.

**Bounded-out adjacent literatures (and why).** A senior reviewer in this area would expect the following five literatures to appear; each is bounded out of v4 with a defense, and seed citations are listed in Section 11.

1. **Mechanism-design and incentive-compatible spatial pricing** (Ma, Fang, Parkes and follow-ons). Bounded out because the review's pricing thread is anchored on the welfare/equilibrium question (does spatial pricing improve total surplus and how is it distributed?), and IC mechanism design answers a different question (does the pricing rule incentivize truthful reporting?). The two are complements rather than substitutes; v5 should add an IC-pricing sub-thread to Theme 4.
2. **Mean-field-game-aware reinforcement learning** (Guo et al.; Saldi; Yang Yaodong's MFG-RL line). Bounded out **partly in error** in v1 — this is the literature that bridges OR equilibrium pricing and DRL control, and Theme 4's controversy claim ("DRL pricing is not in dialogue with OR/economics") is therefore overstated. v3 downgraded Theme 4 from controversy to coverage gap; v4 retains the downgrade. v5 should treat MFG-RL as a sixth thread.
3. **Ride-pooling / shared rides beyond BMG-Q** (Alonso-Mora et al. 2017 PNAS framework and descendants). Bounded out because pooling adds a capacity-constraint dimension orthogonal to the matching/pricing/rebalancing axes that organize this review. Hu 2025 BMG-Q is included because it is RL-side and graph-attention; the optimization-side pooling literature is not, which leaves the BMG-Q comparison "unmoored" until the OR-side pooling literature is added.
4. **Driver labor economics** (Hall, Horton, Knoepfle on Uber labor supply; Cook et al. on the Uber gender earnings gap). Bounded out because the corpus is focused on platform-side control, not driver-side empirical labor questions. The strategic-driver assumption that Theme 4 wants DRL to adopt is empirically grounded in this literature, and v5 should cite at least Hall–Horton–Knoepfle as motivation. Until that addition lands, §8's strategic-driver recommendation is **conditional on v5's driver-labor adds, not unconditional** (see §8 for the softened framing).
5. **ST-GNN demand-forecasting backbone** (DCRNN, Graph WaveNet, STGCN). Bounded out because the review's GNN-encoder discussion (Theme 2) is about *control* representations, not forecasting. The forecasting literature feeds in via UniST/UrbanGPT but is not separately threaded.

**Geographic and platform scope.** The empirical portion of the corpus is heavily concentrated on Didi (Xu 2018, Eshkevari 2022, Yang 2024, Yue 2024, Zhang 2024 NondBREM — five of six deployment/empirical papers), Lyft (Azagirre 2024 — one paper), and NYC-taxi-derived simulators (Lin 2018, Hu 2025, Tresca 2026, Han 2025, Lyu 2025, Yuan 2024, Li 2024). Bolt / FreeNow (EU), Ola (India), Grab / Gojek (Southeast Asia), and Didi-Brazil deployments are not represented. The Castillo welfare result is from a single market (Houston). Where empirical claims are presented in Sections 4–8, this geographic concentration is flagged and claims are scoped accordingly.

## 2. Schema entries (Step 2 product)

The schema below holds, for each paper, the six fields specified in the guide (`population`, `method`, `key_finding`, `effect_size`, `limitations`, `stance`) plus a controlled-vocabulary tag set. Entries are grouped by thread for traceability but are referenced from Section 3 onward by `citation_key` only. Effect sizes that could not be located in the paper's abstract or results are marked `n/a`. Inline `[v3 corrected]` and `[v3 verified]` flags from earlier passes have been pruned from the body text in v4 (the verification provenance is now consolidated in Appendix A); the schema reflects the corrected state. A tag dictionary is provided at the end of this section.

**Stance column convention.** The `stance` field describes what argumentative position the paper takes within the corpus's space of methodological choices (e.g., "supports online RL with safeguards," "contests two-stage as the necessary architecture") rather than evaluating where the paper sits in the literature's reception. v3's entries were inconsistent on this; v4 audits each entry to use the position-taking convention.

## 4. Field overview (background paragraph)

The 2018–2026 ride-hailing ML literature in this corpus has converged on **deep RL with GNN encoders and bipartite-matching combinatorial solvers** as the dominant matching/rebalancing stack, with a fast-growing **LLM/foundation-model** layer on top and a parallel **OR/economics pricing equilibrium** track that has only partially intersected with the ML side within this corpus. The empirical center of gravity in dispatching shifted from single-agent value learning (Xu 2018) to multi-agent RL with mean-field approximations (Li 2019, Hao 2023) and then to graph-attention MARL with grid-as-agent or vehicle-as-agent variants (Wang 2025, Hu 2025). In autonomous mobility-on-demand the bi-level GNN-RL + linear-program template (Gammelli 2021, Gammelli 2023, Singhal 2024, Tresca 2026) is dominant; in deployed dispatch, the production lineage at Didi (Xu 2018, Eshkevari 2022, Yang 2024, Yue 2024) and Lyft (Azagirre 2024) is the strongest empirical base. On the pricing side, the OR/economics line has largely settled the foundational welfare and spatial-pricing questions (Besbes 2021, Afeche 2023, Castillo 2019), while DRL-based joint pricing and matching is represented by a single paper in this corpus (Zhang 2023) — the bounded-out MFG-RL and DRL-pricing literatures listed in Section 11 are exactly where the bridge would come from. The LLM thread is conceptually the broadest but empirically the thinnest: foundation models for urban ST prediction (Yuan 2024, Li 2024) and one peer-reviewed LLM-as-reward-designer (Han 2025) coexist with an LLM-as-controller preprint (Lyu 2025, whose paper-claimed magnitudes include an extraordinary upper end that v4 treats as preliminary) and one peer-reviewed joint matching+repositioning under fairness constraints (Sun 2024).

## 5. Themes (Step 4 product) and MEAL paragraphs (Step 6 product)

This section presents six themes drawn from the matrices: two consensus, **one** controversy, and **three** gap themes. Each theme is one MEAL paragraph (Main idea, Evidence, Analysis, Link), written so that the grammatical subjects of sentences are concepts and findings, not author names. Every theme cites the specific rows in the matrices that support and (where applicable) contradict it. All headline numbers used in this section are verified against the source PDFs (Appendix A).

### 5.1 Theme 1 (consensus, with active contestation) — RL-proposed weights plus a combinatorial matcher is the dominant matching architecture in this corpus, but it is no longer uncontested

**Main idea.** The most common architectural template for matching is "RL learns edge weights on the driver–order bipartite graph; a combinatorial step (Hungarian, ILP, or Gale–Shapley) takes those weights and produces a feasible assignment" — but two recent papers (Yue 2024, Lyu 2025) explicitly contest the template, and the contestation is thin (one peer-reviewed paper on Didi data; one preprint with paper-claimed magnitudes whose upper end is implausible), so the right framing is "dominant template, early contestation" rather than "won." **Evidence.** This template appears in every deployed system in the corpus — Didi's learning + planning approach (Xu 2018), Didi's online state-value iteration (Eshkevari 2022), Lyft's online matching RL (Azagirre 2024) — as well as in the strongest non-deployed graph-MARL baselines (Wang 2025; Hu 2025, where the combinatorial step is an ILP with a learned posterior score). The bi-level AMoD line (Gammelli 2021, Gammelli 2023, Singhal 2024, Tresca 2026) follows the same template with an LP rather than a bipartite-matching CO solver. Two papers contest the template: Yue 2024 ("D2SN") replaces the two-stage pipeline with a single end-to-end DRL model and reports verified 0.7–3.9% TDI gains on Didi data, and Lyu 2025 has the LLM directly emit assignment+reposition actions with paper-claimed gains across fleet sizes (preprint, not peer reviewed; upper end implausible). The TUM hybrid MARL line (Enders 2023: 5% avg / 17% peak; Hoppe 2024: 1.9% vs LRA / 3.5% avg vs greedy) is consistent with the template. **Analysis.** The persistence of the two-stage template is best explained by feasibility: the matching step has hard constraints (one-driver-per-order, geo-fenced batches) that the RL action space cannot easily encode, while the combinatorial solver guarantees feasibility regardless of how noisy or out-of-distribution the learned weights are. End-to-end approaches inherit the burden of feasibility through architectural tricks (autoregressive decoding, hold-actions). **Link.** A current research project in this space should default to the two-stage template unless it has a specific architectural reason to depart from it; if it does depart, it should report the same matching feasibility guarantees the two-stage template provides for free, and benchmark explicitly against Yue 2024's verified 0.7–3.9% TDI as the bar to beat.

### 5.2 Theme 2 (consensus within the corpus reviewed) — graph encoders are the standard state representation for spatial dependence

**Main idea.** Within this corpus, the spatial state of a ride-hailing system is routinely encoded with graph neural networks (GNNs), and the choice of GNN variant — GCN, GAT, MPNN — is treated as a tunable rather than a load-bearing modeling decision. The corpus is not unbiased (the bounded-out ST-GNN forecasting literature is excluded), so the claim is scoped to the corpus rather than to the field as a whole. **Evidence.** The bi-level AMoD line uses graph convolutions (Gammelli 2021), and its 2023 generalization explicitly studies MPNN/GCN/GAT variants and reports that graph-based encoders dominate MLP/CNN baselines via permutation invariance and locality (Gammelli 2023). Graph attention is the most common encoder in current MARL: GAT-MF combines attention with mean field for >3,000 agents (Hao 2023, with the methodological caveat that the 42.7% headline aggregates across heterogeneous benchmarks not all of which are ride-hailing); BMG-Q uses graph-attention DDQN over a localized bipartite match graph (Hu 2025); CoopRide encodes city-scale grid relationships with a GNN (Wang 2025, with the caveat that the 12.4% upper-tail is not a fixed-baseline median). UrbanGPT and UniST treat the spatiotemporal graph as the substrate for foundation-model pretraining (Yuan 2024, Li 2024). The only papers in the corpus that do not use a GNN are the OR/economics pricing papers, which work in continuous space (Besbes 2021, Afeche 2023, Castillo 2019), and the pre-GNN-era MARL formulations (Lin 2018, Li 2019). **Analysis.** Two structural facts make GNNs an attractive default: cities are explicitly graph-structured (zones, road segments, charging stations) and ride-hailing decisions exhibit spatial locality at the right scale for message-passing depth of 1–3 hops. The remaining methodological choice — attention vs. fixed-weight aggregation — appears to matter less than whether some graph encoder is present at all, but this conclusion is sensitive to the corpus selection and may not survive expansion to the ST-GNN forecasting literature where attention vs. convolution debates are active. **Link.** A new ride-hailing controller can adopt graph attention (GAT) or message-passing (MPNN) as the default encoder without a methodological justification beyond "this is the corpus standard"; effort is better spent on the *coordination geometry* (Theme 3) than on the encoder choice.

### 5.3 Theme 3 (controversy) — agent definition: driver/vehicle vs. grid/region

**Main idea.** The corpus is split on whether the basic MARL agent should be a **driver/vehicle** or a **grid/region**, and the choice has cascading consequences for credit assignment, action space size, and how cooperation is modeled. **Evidence.** Driver-as-agent appears in early mean-field MARL (Li 2019), the Lyft and Didi production deployments (Azagirre 2024, Eshkevari 2022), the TUM hybrid MARL line (Enders 2023, Hoppe 2024), the BMG-Q ride-pooling line (Hu 2025), the constrained MFRL line (Jusup 2025), and the LLM controllers (Lyu 2025). Grid-as-agent appears in CoopRide (Wang 2025), GAT-MF's grid-world variant (Hao 2023), Yang 2024's region cooperative game (the ODCMG formulation), and the original contextual MARL (Lin 2018). Production deployments lean driver-as-agent, while the strongest 2024–25 academic MARL benchmarks (Wang 2025) use grid-as-agent. **Analysis.** The driver-as-agent formulation is closer to the operational reality (drivers are the decision-makers) and inherits cleaner credit assignment in the small-fleet regime, but the joint-action space grows with fleet size and forces mean-field or attention-weighted approximations. The grid-as-agent formulation collapses the action space (one action per cell) but loses driver-level personalization and creates a new credit-assignment problem because grid-level rewards do not directly attribute to driver-level decisions. Yang 2024 makes the credit-assignment problem of grid-as-agent explicit (the ODCMG formulation is exactly the question of how a region's value affects others) and proposes goal-reaching collaboration as the answer. CoopRide partially closes the gap by giving each grid both within-grid and cross-grid actions, recovering some driver-level expressivity. **Link.** A research project should pick the agent definition by what its evaluation metric looks like: revenue/GMV-style metrics favor grid-as-agent (cleaner aggregation), while individual-driver metrics (fairness, income variance, acceptance rate) favor driver-as-agent.

### 5.4 Theme 4 (coverage gap, not field controversy) — this corpus does not contain a DRL pricing paper that engages with OR/economics equilibrium results

**Main idea.** *This corpus contains exactly one DRL pricing paper (Zhang 2023), and that paper does not engage with the strategic-driver, welfare-incidence, or equilibrium-anticipating results of the OR/economics pricing line in the corpus.* The right framing is therefore not "the field's DRL pricing is disconnected from OR/economics" — that is a field-level claim that one paper cannot support — but *"this corpus's coverage of DRL pricing is too thin to characterize the field, and the bounded-out MFG-RL literature (§11) is exactly where the bridge would be."* Because the evidence base is too thin to characterize the field, downstream load-bearing claims drawing on Theme 4 (Gap A0 in §7.1; the pricing-project recommendation in §8) are stated as conditional on v5's MFG-RL and DRL-pricing adds rather than as unconditional contributions of v4. **Evidence.** The OR/economics track has shown that local myopic surge is suboptimal under strategic supply (Besbes 2021), that admission control can be optimal under strategic drivers (Afeche 2023), and that surge pricing increases total welfare by 3.53% relative to a uniform-pricing counterfactual on Uber Houston data, with riders gaining 6.97% in surplus and drivers losing 1.97% (Castillo 2019 working paper; Econometrica 2025 published version exists but its numbers were not separately re-checked here). The DRL pricing line is represented in this corpus solely by joint future-aware pricing + matching for ride-pooling (Zhang 2023, verified: average +6.4% revenue / −10.6% vehicle reduction; maxima +17% / −14%) on a NYC simulator, which does not model strategic drivers, does not characterize equilibrium, and does not benchmark against welfare-incidence findings. **Analysis.** A single DRL pricing paper cannot characterize the DRL pricing literature; the field-level controversy framing of v1 and v2 was overstated. The disconnect *as observed in this corpus* is partly a venue mismatch (Econometrica/MS vs. AAAI/KDD) and partly a methodological mismatch (closed-form fluid analysis vs. simulator-based DRL). However, the bounded-out MFG-RL literature (Guo et al.; Yang Yaodong's MFG-RL line; Saldi et al.) has been actively building this bridge for ~5 years, and the additional DRL-pricing seed citations in §11 (Mao et al.; Tang et al.'s pricing/incentive RL work) may already either contest or harden the framing once added. **Link.** A new pricing project should adopt the strategic-driver assumption from the OR/economics literature as a baseline (conditional on v5's driver-labor adds for empirical motivation; see §8) and benchmark its DRL policy against the comparative-static predictions of Besbes 2021 and the welfare decomposition of Castillo 2019 (with Houston/Uber scoping). It should also engage explicitly with the MFG-RL bridge literature rather than treating ML and OR as disconnected. *The specific scoped research question that should anchor such a project is described in Gap A0 (§7.1).*

### 5.5 Theme 5 (gap) — true three-way joint optimization of matching + pricing + rebalancing

**Main idea.** Joint optimization is the visibly emerging frontier, but **no paper in the corpus addresses all three operational levers (matching + pricing + rebalancing) simultaneously with peer-reviewed evidence**. **Evidence.** Pairwise joint optimization exists: matching + repositioning under fairness (Sun 2024 JDRCL); matching + repositioning by LLM agent (Lyu 2025, with paper-claimed magnitudes whose upper end is preliminary); pricing + matching for ride-pooling via ADP (Zhang 2023, verified). The Pavone-lab AMoD line couples matching and rebalancing through bi-level optimization (Gammelli 2021, Singhal 2024, Tresca 2026 with location-specific gap-from-oracle numbers) but treats pricing as exogenous. The MARL lines (Wang 2025, Hao 2023, Hu 2025) treat pricing as exogenous as well. **Analysis.** Two reasons explain the gap. First, the matching/rebalancing levers act on second-to-minute timescales while pricing acts on minute-to-longer timescales; coupling them in a single Markov decision process inflates the state and action spaces and makes off-policy learning unstable. Second, pricing changes demand, which in turn changes the post-matching idle distribution that rebalancing acts on; a three-way joint policy must internalize that feedback, which the equilibrium literature handles via fluid models but DRL has only partially absorbed. JDRCL's max-min fairness convergence guarantee shows that constrained multi-objective MGs can be analyzed in the pairwise case; extending such analysis to three-way is the unsolved technical step. **Link.** A research project that aims to fill this gap should (a) work in the pairwise matching+rebalancing setting first, with a max-min fairness or similar constraint, before adding pricing; (b) borrow the strategic-driver fluid abstraction from Besbes 2021 / Afeche 2023 as the third decision module; and (c) evaluate the joint policy against the welfare incidence of surge documented in Castillo 2019 to test whether three-way joint optimization changes the distributional answer.

### 5.6 Theme 6 (gap, with the v2 counter-reason now in the topic sentence) — LLM role in ride-hailing is unstandardized **but the corpus is also too small to support standardization**

**Main idea.** The LLM/foundation-model thread is the most rapidly growing line in the corpus, but the **role** an LLM plays in the pipeline (encoder, forecaster, reward designer, planner, simulator) is not yet standardized — and with only four LLM/FM papers in the corpus, the lack of standardization is partly a sample-size artifact rather than a deeply structural problem. The premature-consolidation risk is real (a "shared benchmark" with N=4 risks freezing out role choices that have not yet been tried — LLM as simulator, pricing engine, fairness auditor) and is part of the theme statement itself. **Evidence.** Within the corpus, the LLM is a forecaster in UrbanGPT and UniST (Yuan 2024, Li 2024), a reward designer in GARLIC (Han 2025; arithmetically reconciled 2.48 pp empty-load reduction against the best baseline), and a direct planner in LLM-ODDR (Lyu 2025). The first two are pretrained ST foundation models with peer-reviewed cross-city zero/few-shot generalization (UniST 10.1% over no-pretrain; UrbanGPT ~28% over generalization baselines). The third is peer-reviewed at AAAI but uses GPT only to shape rewards, not to act. The fourth (Lyu 2025) gives the LLM the controller role and reports paper-claimed +5.21–48.87% GMV across fleet sizes; **the +48.87% upper end is extraordinary for an unreviewed preprint with a 9× endpoint ratio over a single-paper baseline, has not been replicated, and is treated in v4 as a preliminary upper-bound figure rather than as a representative or headline magnitude**. The paper also reports 1–5 s/decision inference latency, which is a separate deployment concern. **Analysis.** The role mismatch has two practical consequences. First, claims about "LLM for ride-hailing" cannot be compared head-to-head because the LLMs are in different positions in the pipeline; a 28% gain as encoder is not commensurable with a 2.48 pp empty-load reduction as reward designer. Second, no benchmark in the corpus standardizes city, fleet size, and metric the way the AMoD line did with the macroscopic 16-station simulator (Gammelli 2021); the LLM line is therefore at risk of pattern over-fitting on case studies. **However, the right pre-benchmark step is probably a role-comparison study (run the same problem with the LLM in three roles and report) rather than a frozen benchmark, exactly because the role taxonomy itself is not yet stable.** **Link.** A new project should pick a single LLM role (planner, reward designer, or forecaster) and benchmark against a non-LLM baseline of the same operational class; if the project uses an LLM as a controller, latency and grounding (hallucinated assignments) should be measured explicitly, and the Lyu 2025 +48.87% figure should not be treated as the bar to beat. A useful contribution to the field would be a *role-comparison* study, not yet a frozen benchmark.

## 6. Where the synthesis matrix exposed surprises (corpus-specific)

Three patterns surfaced in the matrices that were not visible in the per-paper schema entries.

A first surprise is that **no paper in the corpus uses the rider as the agent**, despite riders being treated as strategic in the OR/economics track (Castillo 2019 estimates rider price-elasticity from data; Besbes 2021 carries strategic riders as a structural feature). The MARL formulations universally agentize either drivers or grids, and the LLM controller (Lyu 2025) agentizes the LLM-controlled vehicle. This is a corpus-specific absence that is invisible in the per-paper schema — every paper has a defensible agent definition for its purpose — but visible only in Matrix 2's lateral view. The absence is consequential for fairness and welfare framings: rider-side metrics (waiting time, surge incidence, allocation equity) are evaluated post-hoc rather than directly optimized.

A second surprise is that **the bi-level paradigm is so dominant in AMoD that the controversy is no longer "RL vs OR" but "which inner problem"**. Gammelli's lab uses an LP for network-flow rebalancing; the TUM lab uses bipartite matching; both share the outer GNN-RL. The methodological agreement is much stronger than a non-specialist would guess from the venue diversity (CDC, ICML, ECC, TCNS, L4DC). Whether this convergence survives the addition of MFG-RL to the corpus (Section 11) is an open question.

A third surprise is that **fairness has only just entered the corpus as a first-class objective**. JDRCL (Sun 2024) gives fairness a max-min formulation with a convergence guarantee; Jusup 2025 gives accessibility the role of a hard constraint; Lyu 2025 includes a fairness-aware dispatching subroutine. Outside these three, fairness in ride-hailing ML is post-hoc or absent. This is a recognizable pattern in ML subfields where a constraint becomes first-class only after the core control problem stabilizes; the literature is at the start of that transition.

### 6.5 Methodological caveats common across the corpus

Per-paper hedges are necessary but insufficient: several methodological issues recur across the corpus and are best discussed once, here, rather than scattered across schema entries. The four caveats below are stated as the reviewer's own concerns, drawing on standard methodological knowledge in the design-of-experiments and offline-RL communities; v3's parenthetical citations to specific design-of-experiments and adaptive-experimentation references have been removed from v4 because those references are not part of the reviewed corpus and should not have appeared as if they were. v5 should add a small set of methodological references to §9 if the caveats below are to be cited rather than stated as concerns.

**Switchback identification under spatial spillovers.** Azagirre 2024 (Lyft) reports its $30M/yr incremental-revenue claim from production switchback experiments. Switchbacks alternate treatment across time blocks within a single market and assume that within-block effects are local and additive across blocks. Both assumptions are stressed in ride-hailing settings: spatial spillovers (a treated block's matching outcomes change the idle distribution that the next block inherits) and time-varying treatment effects (peak-hour treatment is not the same intervention as off-peak treatment) bias estimators in ways the design-of-experiments literature has documented. The Lyft headline number should therefore be read as a bounded estimate with non-trivial residual identification risk, not as a clean causal estimate.

**Off-policy evaluation variance.** Yang 2024 explicitly flags OPE variance at the per-paper level (±7–10pp on per-horizon estimates). Across the corpus, OPE drives several headline numbers (Yang 2024 ~5–6%; Yue 2024 0.7–3.9% TDI). The corpus-wide pattern is that **a non-trivial share of "RL works at scale" claims rest on OPE rather than on prospective deployment**, and OPE estimators are known to be high-variance under distribution shift. Readers should weight prospective-deployment numbers (Xu 2018, Eshkevari 2022, Azagirre 2024) more heavily than OPE numbers when assessing the literature's evidentiary base.

**Publication bias in deployed-RL reports.** Production deployments that fail to ship or are rolled back are not published; the corpus's deployed-system papers (Xu 2018, Eshkevari 2022, Azagirre 2024) are without exception their respective platforms' own success reports. The selection bias is structural — there is no natural way for negative deployed-RL results to enter the literature — but it should temper any reading of "RL works at production scale" that draws on the small set of platform-authored success reports as evidence of generality.

**Concurrent-improvement confounding.** Xu 2018 explicitly notes that the ~10% completion-rate gain it cites came from a *driver-select→platform-assign architectural change*, not from RL itself; the RL-attributable share of the headline is the smaller 0.5–5% revenue figure. This is a corpus-wide concern: production deployments ship many things at once, and percentage gains attributed to "the deployed RL system" often confound RL with concurrent platform improvements. The 2018 Xu paper is unusually clear about this; later deployed-system papers in the corpus are less clear, and the headline percentages should be read with this caveat in mind.

These four caveats apply most directly to the deployed-RL line (Thread A) but also affect the bi-level AMoD line's claim of generalization (Tresca 2026's macro→meso zero-shot gap is large precisely because cross-fidelity transfer is hard) and the LLM line's reported gains (which are case-study-style and not yet evaluated under switchback or OPE scrutiny). The **per-paper methodological notes** added in v4 to Wang 2025 (the "up to 12.4%" upper-tail vs. fixed-baseline median question), Hao 2023 (the cross-task aggregation of the 42.7% headline), and Hu 2025 (the within-paper bias-measurement procedure for >50% overestimation reduction) are paper-specific instances of the same general concern.

## 7. Gap analysis (Step 5 product)

Four candidate gaps are evaluated below against the importance/reasonableness/feasibility criteria. v3 promoted **Gap A0** — *benchmark a DRL three-way controller against Castillo's welfare decomposition* — from v2's §8 into §7 as the load-bearing, scoped, falsifiable research question. v4 retains the promotion but **re-states Gap A0 honestly**: the benchmark is a *structural-alignment* exercise rather than a numerical-replication exercise on Houston, because Houston Uber data are not public and the available substitutes use different data-generating processes. v4 also retains v2/v3's Gap A (three-way joint optimization with strategic-agent guarantees), Gap B (LLM role taxonomy), and Gap C (cross-city transfer / sim-to-real); each retains its v2 counter-reason and verdict.

### 7.1 Gap A0 (load-bearing, v4 re-staged) — structural alignment of a three-way DRL welfare decomposition with Castillo's equilibrium target, on a publicly-available proxy market

*Importance.* This is the single specific research question that every preceding theme of the review converges on: Theme 4's coverage gap identifies the absent DRL/equilibrium bridge, Theme 5's three-way gap names the technical scope of a controller that would close it, and §6.5's methodological caveats specify how the benchmark must be defended. A successful DRL three-way controller whose welfare *structure* aligns with Castillo 2019's three-way decomposition (positive total welfare; positive rider surplus; negative driver surplus, in some specifiable proportion) — even if the absolute numbers differ — would settle whether DRL can match, exceed, or distort the welfare incidence that the OR equilibrium model produced. The importance is therefore not generic ("this has implications for…"); it is the specific empirical-comparison move that the corpus has made possible.

*Reasonableness.* Castillo 2019 published the welfare decomposition that the comparison requires; Sun 2024 (JDRCL) showed that constrained multi-objective MARL is convergent in the pairwise case; Zhang 2023 showed that joint pricing+matching DRL is feasible on a NYC simulator (with verified average +6.4% revenue and −10.6% vehicles). The field cares about welfare incidence (the Castillo paper exists in Econometrica because it answers a load-bearing economics question), and DRL's ability to match welfare-side targets — not just platform-side targets — is exactly what the OR/economics critique was about.

*Feasibility (v4 re-stated honestly).* Houston Uber data are not public, so the benchmark **cannot** test whether a DRL controller produces Castillo's specific Houston numbers. v4 commits to a single concrete substitute: **NYC taxi data with a Houston-calibrated demand-elasticity model derived from Castillo's structural estimates**, with the specific calibration procedure being to fix demand elasticities and entry/hours elasticities at Castillo's reported point estimates and re-fit a NYC market-clearing simulator under those elasticities. This is a *structural-alignment* benchmark — it tests whether the DRL controller's three-way welfare structure (sign and rough proportion) matches Castillo's, **not** whether the DRL controller produces the same numbers, because the underlying market is different. The feasibility budget is 1–2 years for the simulator+controller; the metric structure (total welfare, rider surplus, driver surplus) is decomposable from a DRL controller's simulator outputs given the calibrated elasticities. The pairwise (matching+repositioning) controller (Sun 2024 / Lyu 2025-style) is a feasible starting point; pricing as the third lever can be added on a coarser timescale to manage state-space inflation.

*Counter-reason.* A NYC market under Houston-derived elasticities is neither NYC nor Houston. The benchmark therefore tests whether the DRL controller's welfare structure is *robust to a transplanted demand model*, not whether it matches Castillo's actual Houston market. This is still useful — alignment vs. divergence is an informative result either way, and the *direction-of-effect* finding (does DRL preserve the rider-positive / driver-negative surge incidence?) is a real test of whether DRL approximates the equilibrium welfare structure or distorts it. But the result is weaker than v3's "Houston benchmark" framing implied, and v4 names this explicitly to avoid having it both ways.

*Verdict (v4).* Real, scoped, falsifiable gap, with feasibility re-stated as a structural-alignment exercise on a single specific proxy market (NYC + Houston-derived elasticities) rather than a Houston-specific numerical benchmark. This is the gap a thesis chapter or pilot study should anchor on. The v4 re-statement closes the v3 "had-it-both-ways" weakness the prof flagged.

### 7.2 Gap A — three-way joint optimization with strategic-agent guarantees

*Importance.* Filling this gap meaningfully changes the answer to the core platform-control question, because the equilibrium literature has already shown that pricing and rebalancing are coupled through strategic supply (Besbes 2021, Afeche 2023). A controller that optimizes only matching+rebalancing while taking pricing as exogenous misses the equilibrium feedback that determines welfare incidence (Castillo 2019).

*Reasonableness.* The gap connects to two questions the field cares about: Castillo 2019's distributional result shows that surge has heterogeneous welfare incidence on Uber Houston data, and DRL pricing in this corpus has not engaged with that result; JDRCL's convergence guarantee for two-way joint matching+repositioning under max-min fairness shows that three-way extension is a recognizable technical question in the same lineage.

*Feasibility.* Two-way joint matching+repositioning has been done (JDRCL, Lyu 2025); three-way joint matching+repositioning+pricing requires either (a) coupling a fluid-model pricing module with a DRL matching+repositioning module, or (b) a single MARL formulation with prices as agent actions. (a) is feasible with current methods; (b) is harder but not impossible — the action space inflation is manageable if pricing acts on a coarser timescale.

*Counter-reason* (carried from v2). The OR/economics fluid-model line has, in fact, solved versions of three-way joint optimization analytically (Besbes 2021's network-anticipating pricing implicitly couples spatial pricing with the supply repositioning it induces; Afeche 2023 couples admission control with relocation incentives). The right framing of Gap A may therefore be "the ML literature should adopt the equilibrium framing rather than reinvent three-way coupling in MDPs," not "three-way joint optimization has not been done."

*Verdict (v4).* Real gap on the *implementation-as-DRL* axis (no peer-reviewed three-way DRL paper exists in this corpus); partly closed on the *analytical solvability* axis (OR equilibrium analysis has solved adjacent versions). Gap A0 (§7.1) is the scoped specialization of this gap that v4 anchors on.

### 7.3 Gap B — LLM role taxonomy and shared benchmark

*Importance.* The LLM line is growing fast and the role mismatch (encoder, forecaster, reward designer, planner) makes head-to-head comparison impossible. A standardized comparison is therefore high-importance for any near-term consolidation of the line.

*Reasonableness.* The AMoD subfield's bi-level GNN-RL line consolidated rapidly once Gammelli 2021's macroscopic 16-station benchmark appeared; the LLM line is at the same pre-benchmark stage and would benefit from a similar unifying comparison.

*Feasibility.* A role-comparison study requires (a) a shared simulator/data — most LLM ride-hailing papers already use Manhattan or NYC taxi data, so a NYC-taxi standard is plausible; (b) a shared metric — empty-load rate or GMV, both already widely reported; (c) a shared role-axis — encoder/forecaster/reward-designer/planner. Building a study that fixes (a) and (b) and varies (c) is feasible within a single research project.

*Counter-reason* (carried from v2, also embedded in Theme 6's topic sentence). The LLM line in this corpus is only four papers (Yuan 2024, Li 2024, Han 2025, Lyu 2025); a "shared benchmark" with such a small N risks overfitting the benchmark to the four extant role choices and freezing out roles that have not yet been tried (LLM as simulator, LLM as pricing engine, LLM as fairness auditor). The pre-benchmark stage may be the right stage; consolidation may be premature.

*Verdict (v4).* Real gap, but feasibility-constrained: a benchmark designed too early may calcify the role taxonomy. The best near-term project is a *role-comparison study* (run the same problem with the LLM in three roles and report) rather than a frozen benchmark.

### 7.4 Gap C — cross-city transfer and sim-to-real for production deployment

*Importance.* Production deployments (Xu 2018, Eshkevari 2022, Azagirre 2024) are tied to single platforms. Cross-city transfer determines whether the academic methods (Wang 2025, Hu 2025, Tresca 2026) translate to the next platform without per-city retraining. The importance follows from the asymmetry: there are far more cities than papers.

*Reasonableness.* Cross-city transfer is explicitly demonstrated for the bi-level AMoD line (Gammelli 2021 zero-shot Chengdu↔NYC; Tresca 2026 meta-RL ≈ +61% gain on the Brooklyn special-event scenario). Foundation models in the LLM line claim cross-city zero-shot for forecasting (UniST: MAE 26.84 cross-city; UrbanGPT: ~28% over generalization baselines). The gap is whether these forecasting/rebalancing transfers extend to **deployed matching policies** at production scale, where the production lineage has not reported transfer numbers.

*Feasibility.* Two paths: (a) extend Tresca 2026's meta-RL across truly heterogeneous cities (not just NYC↔Brooklyn↔Shenzhen); (b) use a foundation-model encoder (UniST/UrbanGPT) as the state representation for a matching policy and test whether the foundation-model's cross-city generalization transfers to the policy. Both are within a 1–2 year project.

*Counter-reason* (carried from v2). Cross-city transfer at the *forecasting* layer is likely to be much easier than at the *matching-policy* layer, because matching policies are tightly coupled to platform-specific operational constraints (driver pool composition, regulatory constraints, surge rules, dispatch latency budgets) that are not captured by ST data alone. A foundation-model encoder may transfer cleanly while the policy on top still requires full per-city retraining.

*Verdict (v4).* Real gap on the *full-pipeline transfer* axis; the *encoder-only transfer* version of the gap is already partly closed by UniST and UrbanGPT.

A fifth candidate gap — "fairness as a hard constraint" — partially fails the *reasonableness* test because the field is already moving in this direction (JDRCL, Jusup 2025, Lyu 2025). It is more accurate to call fairness an emerging consensus rather than a gap. A sixth candidate — "cross-city / population not yet studied" — fails the *importance* test as written; "population X has not been studied" is the bad-gap framing flagged by the guide.

## 8. Operational implications for a research project entering this field

The synthesis above implies five concrete defaults for a new research project in this space; the load-bearing research question is in §7.1.

A project should **default to the two-stage RL+combinatorial template** for matching unless it has an architectural reason to depart from it; if it does, it must show how feasibility is preserved (Theme 1). The bar to beat for end-to-end alternatives is Yue 2024's verified 0.7–3.9% TDI on Didi data (not Lyu 2025's paper-claimed +48.87% upper end, which is preliminary and should not be used as a benchmark).

A project should **default to a graph encoder (GAT or MPNN)** for spatial state, and spend its modeling budget on the coordination geometry (driver-as-agent vs grid-as-agent) rather than the encoder choice (Themes 2 and 3). The encoder-default conclusion is scoped to the corpus reviewed and may not survive expansion to the ST-GNN forecasting literature.

A project working on dispatching at scale should **pre-decide** between driver-as-agent (cleaner driver-level metrics, harder credit assignment) and grid-as-agent (cleaner aggregate metrics, harder driver attribution) based on the metric of interest (Theme 3).

A project working on pricing should **consider strategic-driver assumptions as a baseline structural choice** and benchmark against the comparative-static predictions of Besbes 2021 and the welfare decomposition of Castillo 2019 (with Houston/Uber scoping and the citation-hygiene flag from Appendix B). It should also engage with the bounded-out MFG-RL literature as the bridge between OR equilibrium and DRL control (Section 11), not treat the two as disconnected (Theme 4). **The strategic-driver assumption is empirically grounded in the bounded-out driver-labor literature (Hall–Horton–Knoepfle; Cook et al.; see §11), which v5 must add for the recommendation to rest on non-circular evidence. Until v5 lands the driver-labor adds, this recommendation should be read as conditional on the v5 expansion, not as an unconditional prescription on the strength of OR papers (Besbes 2021; Afeche 2023) that themselves assume strategic drivers.**

A project working on LLMs in ride-hailing should **pick one LLM role and one non-LLM baseline of the same operational class**, and report inference latency and grounding behavior; "LLM is the controller" with a 1–5 s decision latency is not a deployment-ready claim (Theme 6). The Lyu 2025 +48.87% paper-claimed upper end should not be treated as the bar to beat; downweight that figure when comparing across LLM roles. A *role-comparison study* (one LLM, three roles, one problem) is the right pre-benchmark contribution.

Production-deployment claims should be evaluated against the §6.5 caveats (switchback identification, OPE variance, publication bias, concurrent-improvement confounding) rather than taken at face value; the headline percentages in this corpus are bounded estimates, not clean causal estimates.
