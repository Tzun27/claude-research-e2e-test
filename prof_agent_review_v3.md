# Senior Reviewer's Assessment of `literature_review_ride_hailing_ml_2018_2026_v3.md`

*Reviewer: senior academic referee, posture: skeptical-but-fair. Reviewed against the actual papers in the same directory and against the v2 review's punch list.*

---

## 1. Overall assessment

v3 is a substantial improvement over v2 on the verification axis. The fabricated framework name (FOVIR → ODCMG) is corrected globally, the three "(unverified)" numbers v2 flagged are re-extracted with proper statistical decomposition (average vs. peak; per-baseline rather than range), the Tresca 2026 misattributions are fixed, the GARLIC arithmetic is reconciled, the Castillo citation hygiene is flagged, the §6.5 methodological-caveats section is added, and Gap A0 is correctly promoted to §7.1 — every individual item on the v2 punch list that could be closed by re-reading the PDFs has been closed. **However, the review remains at major-revision status** because the underlying scope problems v2 also raised — corpus size below the author's own 30–50 target, MFG-RL still bounded out (Theme 4's framing depends on this literature's absence), several venue attributions still labeled "pending confirmation" in April 2026, the strategic-driver recommendation in §8 still methodologically circular — are acknowledged in v3's §1.1, §5.4, §8, and §11 but deferred rather than fixed. v3 has corrected the things the author could correct without doing additional research; what is owed v4 is the additional research itself, especially the corpus expansion that two of the six themes structurally require. As a thesis-chapter draft this is now defensible as a *verified* survey of a deliberately-scoped corpus; as a literature review that supports the field-level theme claims it makes, it is not yet there.

---

## 2. Major issues, ordered by severity

### (A) Scope problems were diagnosed but not fixed; v3 inherits v2's structural limits

v2's central scope critique was that 27 papers sits below the author's own 30–50 target, that MFG-RL was bounded out "partly in error," and that Theme 4 ("DRL pricing does not engage with OR/economics") is anchored on a single DRL pricing paper (Zhang 2023). v3 acknowledges every one of these critiques — in §1.1, §5.4 ("downgraded from controversy to coverage gap in this corpus"), §6, and §11 — but acknowledgment is not a fix. The corpus is still 27 papers; MFG-RL is still bounded out; Theme 4 still rests on the same n=1 DRL pricing base. The downgrade from "controversy" to "coverage gap" softens the framing without fixing the underlying evidentiary gap, and Theme 4 is still doing load-bearing work for both Gap A0 (§7.1) and §8's pricing recommendations. A revision that flags but does not fix its own structural problem is a revision in the bibliographic sense, not the substantive sense. The §11 expansion plan is the right plan; v4 should not appear without it actually landed.

### (B) Three venue attributions remain "pending confirmation" in April 2026

Sun 2024 (JDRCL) is cited as "TKDE 2024 (KDD'22 ext.; venue confirmation pending)"; Tresca 2026 as "TCNS 2026 (in press, venue confirmation pending)"; and Castillo 2019/2025 is flagged because "the PDF in the directory is the December 2019 working-paper version" while the published Econometrica vol. 93 (2025) version's numbers were "not separately verified." The current date is April 2026 and all three attributions are now testable. Citing a venue with a "pending" hedge is not defensible scholarly practice — either the venue is confirmed and cited, or the work is cited as preprint/working paper. The Castillo case is the most serious of the three because the welfare numbers anchor Theme 4, Gap A0, and §8: a load-bearing claim should not rest on a paper version the reviewer admits has not been re-verified against its published form.

### (C) §6.5's invocation of methodological literature lacks bibliographic backing

§6.5 is a strong conceptual addition — switchback identification under spillovers, OPE variance, publication bias in deployed-RL reports, and concurrent-improvement confounding are exactly the corpus-wide caveats v2 flagged as missing. But the section cites Bojinov & Shephard on adaptive experimentation and Glynn–Johari–Rasouli on switchback identification under interference parenthetically, and neither appears in the §9 reference table or in the schema. Either these references should be added to the corpus and threaded through Theme 1 and §8's reading of the deployed-system evidence (which would also address part of the size shortfall), or the parenthetical citations should be removed and the methodological points stated as the author's own acknowledged concerns. As written, §6.5 borrows authority from a literature it does not actually engage with.

### (D) The Lyu 2025 (LLM-ODDR) headline range is uncritically reproduced

"+5.21% (small fleet) to +48.87% (large fleet) GMV" is reported in Theme 6, Matrix 5, the reference table, and Appendix A. A 48.87% GMV improvement over "traditional RL" on a large-fleet ride-hailing setting is an extraordinary claim for an unreviewed preprint, and Appendix A logs only that the numbers are verified against the source's Table I — not that the experimental setup, baselines, or simulator are credible enough to support that magnitude. The review qualifies the paper as a preprint with 1–5 s/decision latency in passing but does not draw the obvious skeptical inference that an extraordinary range from an unreviewed preprint should not be a headline number. A senior reviewer would put substantially more weight on the implausibility of the upper end.

### (E) Gap A0's feasibility argument walks back its own framing

§7.1 promotes "benchmark a DRL three-way controller against Castillo's welfare decomposition on the Houston market" as the load-bearing scoped, falsifiable research question. The *feasibility* paragraph then concedes that Houston Uber data are not public and proposes three substitutes: a public Houston taxi dataset, NYC taxi data with a Houston-calibrated demand model, or a synthetic model from Castillo's structural estimates. None of these is "the Houston market" — each substitutes a different data-generating process. The *counter-reason* paragraph then admits this directly: "the benchmark therefore tests whether the DRL controller's welfare structure aligns with the equilibrium-model's structure, not whether the DRL controller produces the same numbers." Read together, §7.1 promises a Houston benchmark and then immediately walks back to a structural-alignment exercise. Either the gap should be re-stated honestly as "structural alignment of a three-way DRL welfare decomposition with the equilibrium target" — without Houston as a load-bearing testbed — or the feasibility section should commit to one specific dataset and one specific calibration procedure. The current framing has it both ways and is the weakest part of v3's gap analysis.

### (F) §8's "adopt strategic agents" recommendation is acknowledged as circular but not fixed

§8 paragraph 4 explicitly says: "without [empirical backing from the bounded-out driver-labor literature], the recommendation is methodologically circular." The driver-labor papers (Hall–Horton–Knoepfle; Cook et al.) are in §11 as v4 adds. The acknowledgment is honest, but a recommendation that the author admits is methodologically circular should not survive in v3 — it should be qualified out, not flagged for v4 fix. As written, §8 recommends adopting strategic-driver assumptions on the strength of OR papers (Besbes 2021; Afeche 2023) that themselves assume strategic drivers. This is the same critique v2 made, restated with the same response. A reviewer would expect at minimum a softened version: "consider strategic-driver assumptions as a baseline only when the driver-labor empirical evidence the §11 list cites is added; until then, treat the recommendation as conditional."

### (G) Theme 4's downgrade obscures the residual load-bearing role it still plays

v3's framing of Theme 4 as "this corpus does not contain a DRL pricing paper that engages with OR/economics equilibrium results" is the right corpus-coverage statement, but the theme is still being used as load-bearing input to Gap A0 (§7.1, "Theme 4's coverage gap identifies the absent DRL/equilibrium bridge") and to §8 (the pricing-project paragraph). If the evidence base is too thin to characterize the field, then downstream claims drawing on that evidence base should also be qualified, not only the theme statement. The §8 recommendation to "engage with the bounded-out MFG-RL literature" is correct, but it is being made on the basis of a coverage gap in the reviewer's own corpus rather than on a substantive finding about the literature's actual state. Without v4's MFG-RL adds, this is a thin foundation for a load-bearing recommendation.

### (H) Critical engagement with cited papers' internal methodology is uneven

§6.5 covers four corpus-wide caveats well, but several individual papers invite specific methodological scrutiny that v3 does not provide. Wang 2025 (CoopRide)'s "up to 12.4% over reported baselines" is reported without scrutiny of which baselines, on what fraction of experiments, or whether 12.4% is a representative number or an upper-tail. Hao 2023's 42.7% headline is qualified as "across heterogeneous benchmarks not all of which are ride-hailing," but the question of how much of the headline is ride-hailing-attributable is not asked. Hu 2025's ">50% overestimation reduction" is a strong claim about a Q-learning pathology and deserves explicit scrutiny of the bias-measurement procedure. The pattern is that v3 has done a careful pass on numeric extraction but not on whether the extracted numbers should have been the headline in the first place.

### (I) The §0 + v3 changelog occupies the document's opening real estate

Roughly 800 words of meta-commentary precede the substantive research question. This is appropriate in a response-to-reviewers letter but excessive in a literature review intended to stand on its own. A reader looking for the question must scroll past corrections, sub-corrections, and corrections-to-the-changelog-process. The changelog should be moved to an appendix (separate from Appendix A's verification log) or to a separate response-to-reviewers document. A reviewer reading the document fresh would form the impression that v3 is more concerned with defending against v2's review than with framing its own contribution.

## 3. Minor issues (grouped)

The "27 papers" count requires explicit handling of the Sun 2024 cross-listing (Thread B placement; Thread E cross-reference): if Sun 2024 is counted in both threads the threadwise sum is 27, if only in Thread B the sum is 26 unique. v3 still adds to 27 only with the cross-listing convention; this should be stated. The "stance" column in the schema is filled inconsistently — some entries describe what the paper argues, others evaluate where it sits in the literature; a one-sentence style note would help. The "Castillo2019/2025" citation key is unusual scholarly practice; standard convention is to cite the version actually read with a "see also" to the published version, not to slash the two years together. The §10 audit checklist contains both externally-verifiable and self-attested items mixed together (e.g., "MEAL paragraphs: each theme paragraph has identifiable M, E, A, L" is pure self-attestation, while "every headline number has a corresponding row in Appendix A" is verifiable). A two-column format would make the distinction explicit. The "within the corpus reviewed" hedge is described in §10 as "pruned from paragraphs where Section 1.1's scope is already evident," but it still appears in Themes 2 and 4 and several §3 comparability notes; the document is tonally inconsistent on this. The "Tag dictionary 51 distinct tags" claim in §2.6 is presented as a v3 correction of v2's 52, but the count itself is not auditable from the listing without a tag-by-tag enumeration; readers who try will note that the per-axis counts (15 + 5 + 10 + 8 + 3 + 10 = 51) check out, but a parenthetical sum would prevent the next reviewer from re-doing the check. The phrase "v3 corrected" appears so often inline that it functions as a verbal tic; once §0 has documented the correction process, the inline flags can be removed from the body.

## 4. Suggestions for revision, in priority order

1. **Land the §11 expansion before the next pass.** Highest priority is the MFG-RL line (Guo et al.; Cui & Koeppl; the Yang Yaodong line) and at least two additional DRL-pricing papers (Mao et al.; Tang et al.'s Didi pricing/incentive RL work), because Theme 4's framing depends on whether the bridge literature exists. Second priority is Tang et al. KDD 2019 (the multi-driver order-dispatching paper that bridges Xu 2018 to Eshkevari 2022, which Thread A's narrative currently jumps over). After expansion, re-derive Themes 4 and 6 from the new evidence base.
2. **Verify the three "pending venue" attributions.** Sun 2024 (TKDE), Tresca 2026 (TCNS), and especially Castillo 2025 (Econometrica) are all testable in April 2026. Re-verify Castillo's published-version numbers against the working-paper numbers — counterfactual estimates often shift between drafts, and the Theme 4 / Gap A0 / §8 stack relies on these numbers being right.
3. **Cite the methodological literature §6.5 invokes.** Add Bojinov & Shephard, Glynn–Johari–Rasouli (or the precise references for switchback identification under interference) to the §9 reference table, and thread them through Theme 1 and §8's reading of the deployed-RL evidence. This both honors the citations and addresses ~3–5 papers of the corpus-size shortfall.
4. **Re-stage Gap A0.** Either commit to one specific dataset and one specific calibration procedure for the "Houston benchmark," or re-frame the gap as "structural alignment of DRL three-way welfare decomposition with the equilibrium target" without Houston as load-bearing. The current "Houston benchmark, but not really" framing has it both ways.
5. **Apply skepticism to the LLM-ODDR magnitude.** The +48.87% upper bound from an unreviewed preprint deserves at minimum a "the upper end of this range is implausibly large for the reported setup; replication has not been attempted; downweight when comparing across LLM roles" caveat in the schema entry, Matrix 5, and Theme 6. Do not let a paper-claimed range with a 9× ratio between endpoints serve as a headline.
6. **Soften §8's strategic-driver recommendation** to be conditional on the v4 driver-labor adds, or remove it from §8 until v4 lands. A recommendation the author already concedes is methodologically circular should not be in operational implications.
7. **Move §0 and the v3 changelog blurb out of the manuscript body** into an appendix or a separate response-to-reviewers document. Open with §1's research question.
8. **Add per-paper methodological notes for the headline numbers that deserve scrutiny:** Wang 2025's "up to 12.4%" baseline composition; Hao 2023's ride-hailing-attributable share of 42.7%; Hu 2025's overestimation-reduction measurement procedure.
9. **Replace the "27 papers" count with a clean accounting** that explicitly handles the Sun 2024 cross-listing, and audit the schema's "stance" column for consistent semantics.

## 5. What the review does well (preserve in revision)

The verification work in v3 is genuinely strong and should not be undone. Appendix A's per-claim `verified_against_pdf` log is exactly the right format for a literature review that has previously suffered from extraction errors, and the catch on FOVIR vs ODCMG is the kind of correction that takes real care. The Enders 2023 / Hoppe 2024 statistical-object distinction (average vs peak; per-baseline rather than range) is the right level of precision; many published reviews would have left the v2 framing in place. The GARLIC arithmetic check (40.71 − 38.23 = 2.48 pp) is a careful-reader move and the right response (report only the reconciled figure, flag the unreconciled one) is the right one. §6.5's four-caveat synthesis (switchback identification, OPE variance, publication bias, concurrent-improvement confounding) is a real contribution that v2 lacked, and Matrix 2's lateral observation that "no paper agentizes the rider" is exactly the kind of corpus-specific finding that justifies the matrix-as-method approach. The MEAL paragraphs in §5 are correctly structured — concept-as-grammatical-subject, identifiable M/E/A/L — and the v3 Theme 4 downgrade, while not a full fix, is honest in the right direction. The §11 expansion plan is concrete, named, and falsifiable, which is rare in revision plans. None of this should be undone in v4; the structural fixes named above are additions, not replacements.
